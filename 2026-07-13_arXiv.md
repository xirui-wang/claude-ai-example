## Extended Summaries: 2D Materials & Microwave Engineering

### [2607.08710] Competing Chern states revealed by quasiparticle charging in moiré rhombohedral graphene
Moiré rhombohedral graphene (mRG) has emerged as a premier platform for fractional Chern insulators (FCIs), but an unresolved debate concerned whether the low-temperature evolution from FCIs to a quantum anomalous Hall (QAH) background reflected edge-state non-equilibration or a genuine bulk phase transition. Li, Geng, Seo, Mak and collaborators address this with two complementary bulk-sensitive probes: quasiparticle tunneling into a mesoscopic mRG antidot device and bulk resistance measurements near ν = 2/3. The antidot tunneling data reveal quasiparticles carrying exactly one electron charge at both ν = 1 and ν = 2/3, ruling out fractional-charge signatures expected for an FCI phase at low temperatures. Temperature-dependent bulk resistance near ν = 2/3 further indicates a thermodynamic phase transition from an FCI to a generalized anomalous Hall crystal (gAHC), directly implicating a bulk mechanism rather than edge-state physics. This work provides the most direct experimental evidence to date for competing Chern states and thermodynamic phase transitions in 2D moiré graphene systems, with broad implications for understanding many-body topology in flat-band materials.

### [2607.06711] Moiré Phonon Condensation in Magic-Angle Twisted Bilayer Graphene
The structural morphology of magic-angle twisted bilayer graphene (TBG) undergoes a dramatic crossover from weak breathing corrugation at large twist angles to large-amplitude bending corrugation near θ ≈ 1.08°, but the microscopic origin of this collective transition remained elusive. Zhouyin, Jiang, Kong, and Guo show that this crossover is a phonon soft-mode condensation: the layer-symmetric A₁ moiré flexural phonon branch progressively softens on the breathing mode branch as the twist angle decreases toward the magic angle, eventually freezing into the large-amplitude bending morphology. At θ = 1.08°, only two A₁ phonon modes are needed to account for more than 99.5% of the spectral weight of all 11,164 atoms in the moiré supercell—an extraordinary compression of degrees of freedom. This Moiré Phonon Condensation (MPC) mechanism provides a unified phononic picture of TBG structural reconstruction and suggests that the same physics governs other moiré 2D materials near their magic angles.

### [2607.08618] Harnessing orbital Hall effect for energy-efficient magnetization switching in room-temperature van der Waals ferromagnet Fe₃GaTe₂
The 2D van der Waals ferromagnet Fe₃GaTe₂ has attracted intense interest for room-temperature spintronic applications due to its large magnetocrystalline anisotropy, but efficient and low-power electrical control of its magnetization has remained a challenge. Zhang, Bai, Pu, and Yang demonstrate that using Cr—a light metal with large orbital Hall conductivity—as the orbital-current source achieves significantly more energy-efficient spin-orbit torque (SOT) switching of Fe₃GaTe₂ than conventional heavy-metal spin Hall sources such as Pt or Ta. The orbital Hall effect in Cr generates an orbital current that, upon interfacial conversion to spin angular momentum in Fe₃GaTe₂, provides a higher effective torque efficiency and lower switching current density. This work establishes the orbital Hall effect as a viable and superior mechanism for controlling 2D vdW magnets, opening a route toward ultra-low-power SOT-MRAM based on all-2D heterostructures.

### [2607.07642] Acoustic-phonon-driven spin-lattice relaxation of the hBN boron vacancy in the sub-THz regime
The negatively charged boron vacancy (V_B⁻) in hexagonal boron nitride is a leading solid-state qubit platform for quantum sensing, but its longitudinal spin relaxation time T₁ is substantially shorter than desired, particularly at high magnetic fields where the relevant Zeeman transitions lie far below the energies of optical phonons. Adhikary and Upadhyaya apply a first-principles acoustic-mode spin-phonon relaxation theory to V_B⁻ and demonstrate quantitative agreement with experimental T₁ vs. magnetic field and temperature data without any free parameters. The key finding is that relaxation is dominated by a direct one-phonon process resonant with the Zeeman splitting, and that the dominant decoherence channel is the out-of-plane flexural acoustic (ZA) phonon branch—a phonon mode unique to 2D van der Waals hosts that has no analogue in bulk 3D defect systems. This result identifies a fundamental constraint imposed by the 2D nature of hBN on spin coherence and points toward engineering the phonon bath (e.g., through substrate coupling or isotopic purification) to extend T₁ for quantum-sensing applications.

### [2607.00490] A Versatile Analytical Model for Fast and Accurate Determination of Feedline-Coupled Resonators for Superconducting Qubit Readout
Accurate knowledge of resonance frequencies and coupling quality factors (Q_c) is critical for designing superconducting qubit readout circuits, yet established finite-element methods are computationally expensive for large chip layouts. Luo, Richard, Tsitsilin and colleagues introduce a closed-form analytical model that combines four-port microwave network analysis with conformal mapping of edge-coupled coplanar waveguide (CPW) geometry to compute even- and odd-mode impedances for feedline-coupled λ/4 resonators. The model is applicable to both planar 2D chip architectures and 3D heterogeneous packages, making it broadly useful for state-of-the-art quantum processor designs. Validation against cryogenic measurements of a test chip with resonators of varying dimensions, as well as against FEM simulations, confirms that the analytical model matches experimental data at a fraction of the computational cost. By enabling rapid resonator parameter sweeps during chip design, this work streamlines the microwave engineering workflow for superconducting quantum hardware.

### [2607.06995] Surface-Reconstruction-Driven Insulating Behavior in Metallic Charge-Density-Wave 1T-TaSe₂
The 2D charge-density-wave material 1T-TaSe₂ presents a long-standing puzzle: its bulk is metallic, yet its surface consistently shows an insulating gap, traditionally attributed to a surface Mott insulator driven by enhanced electron correlations. Lee and Cho challenge this interpretation using density functional theory: they find that the surface instead adopts a bilayer CDW stacking configuration (in contrast to the bulk's single-layer stacking), and that interlayer hybridization of Ta 5d_{z²} orbitals in the bilayer geometry opens a ~0.4 eV gap without requiring any on-site Coulomb repulsion—a pure band insulator driven by structural reconstruction. This result has significant implications for interpreting transport, tunneling, and ARPES measurements on TMD surfaces and demonstrates that CDW stacking reconstruction can be a dominant mechanism for surface electronic phase transitions in 2D layered materials.
