## Extended Summaries: 2D Materials & Microwave Engineering

### Property Prediction of Stacked Bilayer Materials: A Multimodal Learning Approach ([2606.01012](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.01012))
**2D Materials.** The design space of two-dimensional (2D) bilayer heterostructures is vast, as arbitrary stacking of different monolayers can give rise to emergent interfacial properties not present in either constituent layer. Vuong et al. address this challenge by developing a multimodal learning framework that jointly encodes structural and chemical representations of both component layers along with their stacking configuration to predict properties arising at the interface. The approach was evaluated on several 2D bilayer datasets and achieves accuracy superior to single-modality baselines. By accelerating property screening for 2D bilayer combinations, this work substantially expands the toolkit for rational materials discovery in van der Waals heterostructure engineering. The paper was accepted at the International Joint Conference on Artificial Intelligence (IJCAI 2026).

### Absolute Measurement of Penetration Depth of Superconducting Thin Films Using Microwave Stripline Resonators ([2605.28759](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.28759))
**Microwave Engineering.** The London penetration depth λ is one of the most fundamental parameters of a superconductor, encoding the superfluid density and pairing strength, yet its absolute measurement in thin films has historically been challenging. Dutta et al. present a hybrid experimental–computational scheme in which a superconducting stripline resonator is fabricated on or adjacent to the film of interest, and the resonant frequency shift due to kinetic inductance is tracked as a function of temperature. Finite-element electromagnetic simulations in COMSOL Multiphysics are used to deconvolve geometric factors, yielding an absolute λ(T) without calibration against a reference sample. The technique is broadly applicable to any superconducting thin-film geometry and is particularly relevant for characterizing films destined for microwave quantum devices such as qubits, resonators, and kinetic inductance detectors.

### Microwave Crosstalk in Planar Superconducting Quantum Devices ([2606.02440](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.02440))
**Microwave Engineering.** As superconducting quantum processors scale toward hundreds of qubits, microwave signal crosstalk between neighboring control lines and circuit elements becomes a critical limiting factor for gate fidelity. Song et al. identify two dominant geometric structures responsible for strong crosstalk: (1) a drive line routed in close proximity to a neighboring qubit, and (2) a drive line crossing a qubit–qubit coupler via an air bridge. By designing and characterizing test devices that isolate each structure, they develop physical electromagnetic models that quantitatively reproduce the measured crosstalk spectra, providing experimentally validated design rules. The work from Wallraff's group at ETH Zurich offers a prescriptive framework for layout engineers seeking to suppress parasitic coupling in large-scale superconducting quantum processors.
