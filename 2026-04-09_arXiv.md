## Extended Summaries: 2D Materials & Microwave Engineering

### Engineering 2D High-Temperature Ferromagnets in Alkali-Decorated Tetragonal CoSe Monolayer ([2604.04739](https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.04739))
Using density functional theory calculations including spin-orbit coupling, Peng and co-workers demonstrate that decorating a tetragonal CoSe monolayer with alkali atoms (Li, Na, K, Rb, Cs) induces spontaneous ferromagnetism with strong in-plane magnetic anisotropy. The most remarkable candidate, NaCoSe, achieves a magnetic anisotropy energy exceeding 800 μeV/Co and a Curie temperature well above 300 K, both among the highest predicted for 2D magnets. LiCoSe is identified as a half-metal, making it attractive for spintronic applications. The tunability via strain modulation further allows Tc adjustment without compositional changes. This work expands the library of theoretically predicted stable 2D ferromagnets with room-temperature functionality, relevant to next-generation ultrathin spintronic and magnonic devices.

### Moiré Topological Magnetism Twist-Engineered from 2D Spin Spirals ([2603.04620](https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.04620))
This work proposes a field-free route to topological magnetic textures by twisting two antiferromagnetic monolayers—NiCl₂ or NiBr₂—relative to each other. In each monolayer, locked commensurate spin spirals in the two sublattices form, and upon twisting they create spatially alternating ferromagnetic and antiferromagnetic moiré domains that spontaneously stabilize topological magnetism. For twisted NiCl₂, the twist angle continuously tunes between isolated and high-order antiferromagnetic bimerons; for NiBr₂, additional vertical compressive strain transforms trivial triple-q spin spirals into moiré topological states. The approach requires no external magnetic field or doping, offering a clean experimental knob—twist angle—to engineer topological spin textures in van der Waals magnets. These results suggest that twistronics can be extended from electronic to magnetic topology.

### Influence of Sulphur Vacancies on Ultrafast Charge Separation in WS₂-Graphene Heterostructures ([2603.16247](https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.16247))
Using time- and angle-resolved photoemission spectroscopy (trARPES), this study maps how sulphur vacancy concentration controls interlayer charge dynamics in WS₂/graphene heterostructures. Increasing vacancy density prolongs the WS₂ conduction-band electron lifetime but shortens the lifetime of the interlayer charge-separated state, attributed to shifts in the energy alignment between vacancy trap states and graphene's Dirac point. ARPES confirms that vacancies both modify the band alignment and introduce additional doping. A rate-equation model extracts an electron tunneling time from sulphur-vacancy states into graphene's Dirac cone of ~4 ps. These results directly quantify how atomically controlled defect engineering tunes the ultrafast photophysics of TMD-graphene heterostructures, with implications for photodetectors and photovoltaic devices based on 2D interfaces.

### Strongly Correlated Superconductivity in Twisted Bilayer Graphene: A Gutzwiller Study ([2604.04631](https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.04631))
Liang and co-workers apply a Gutzwiller variational wavefunction—allowing the projector to break charge U(1) symmetry—to an 8-band model of magic-angle twisted bilayer graphene (MATBG) at ν = 2.5 filling. The ground-state phase diagram as a function of Hubbard U and Hund's coupling J_A reveals a superconducting dome that separates a weakly correlated BCS-like phase at small U from a strongly correlated phase at large U, with the latter characterized by strong suppression of f-orbital charge fluctuations while maintaining finite pairing amplitude and quasiparticle coherence. At the crossover boundary, a novel small Fermi liquid state emerges with Fermi surface volume equal to ν+2, reminiscent of the pseudogap phenomenology in cuprates. The framework demonstrates that Gutzwiller-level treatment captures the interplay of Mott physics and pairing without invoking spin-liquid intermediate states, and is directly applicable to other 2D correlated superconductors such as moiré TMDs.

### A Plug-and-Play Superconducting Quantum Controller at Millikelvin Temperatures ([2604.05693](https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.05693))
Liu and co-workers present a Josephson-junction-based superconducting quantum controller chip that operates at 10 mK and connects directly to transmon qubits via flip-chip bonding, eliminating the room-temperature microwave electronics chain that limits scalability in conventional architectures. The controller performs all-digital single-qubit gates using on-chip microwave pulse generation with programmable amplitudes and phases, achieving an average Clifford gate fidelity of 99.9% with leakage to higher transmon levels at the 10⁻⁴ level, and an estimated gate energy of only 0.121 fJ per operation. The design exploits the intrinsic low-loss and low-noise environment of the millikelvin stage to suppress cross-talk and thermal noise, with the Josephson nonlinearity providing signal gain without added thermal photons. This millikelvin integration approach addresses a key scalability bottleneck in superconducting quantum computing, and the chip-to-chip interconnect architecture is compatible with modular multi-processor quantum systems.
