## Extended Summaries: 2D Materials & Microwave Engineering

### [Nature Materials] Atomically precise synthesis and simultaneous heterostructure integration of 2D transition metal dichalcogenides through nano-confinement

Controlling the morphology and cleanliness of 2D TMD monolayers during synthesis remains a central challenge for scalable device fabrication. This paper demonstrates that placing a graphene or h-BN capping layer on a target substrate creates a nano-confined CVD environment that precisely directs growth kinetics, yielding TMD monolayers with tailored morphologies and atomically sharp edges. Crucially, the same nano-confinement simultaneously forms ultraclean vdW interfaces between the newly grown TMD and the capping layer. As a demonstration, Janus S–Mo–Se monolayers are synthesized via bottom-plane chalcogen substitution within the confined geometry, with atomic precision confirmed by electron microscopy. The approach generalizes beyond MoSe₂ and opens a scalable, contamination-free route to complex heterostructure assembly directly during synthesis.

### [Nature Materials] Orbital-hybridization-induced Ising-type superconductivity in a confined gallium layer

Two-dimensional Ising superconductors have so far relied primarily on strong spin-orbit coupling from heavy transition metals; this paper extends the concept to a confined gallium trilayer grown between a graphene electrode and a 6H-SiC(0001) substrate. Angle-resolved photoemission and transport measurements show that orbital hybridization at the gallium–substrate interface locks spins out-of-plane via Ising-type coupling, yielding an in-plane upper critical field of ~21.98 T at 400 mK — 3.38 times the Pauli paramagnetic limit. This represents a qualitatively different pathway to Ising superconductivity that bypasses the need for heavy transition metals, suggesting that orbital engineering at buried interfaces can be used broadly to stabilize 2D Ising pairing in light-element materials.

### [Physical Review Letters 136, 156501] Momentum-resolved spectroscopy of 2D superconductors with the quantum twisting microscope

The quantum twisting microscope (QTM) places a twisted graphene tip over a 2D sample and uses in-plane momentum conservation during tunnelling to perform momentum-resolved spectroscopy. In this PRL, the QTM directly measures the Bogoliubov coherence factors — the amplitude of electron and hole components — of quasiparticle excitations in a 2D superconductor, as well as the momentum-dependent pairing amplitude, quantities that were previously inaccessible by conventional STM. The approach provides the first direct momentum-space map of the superconducting gap and coherence factors in a 2D material, opening a new experimental window into unconventional pairing symmetries, anisotropic gaps, and vortex physics in vdW superconductors and moiré systems.
