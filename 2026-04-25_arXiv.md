## Extended Summaries: 2D Materials & Microwave Engineering

### Cryogenic shock exfoliation for ultrahigh mobility rhombohedral graphite nanoelectronics ([2604.21912](https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.21912))

Rhombohedral multilayer graphene (RMG) is a highly tunable platform for correlated electron physics — hosting field-effect-controllable magnetic, superconducting, and topological phases — but progress has been limited by the scarcity of rhombohedral stacking in natural graphite. This work introduces *cryogenic shock exfoliation*, a novel mechanical protocol that exploits liquid-nitrogen thermal shock to preferentially cleave graphite along rhombohedral grain boundaries, yielding large-area (>1300 μm²) RMG flakes with a device fabrication yield of 90%. Combined with a low-pressure van der Waals assembly technique that preserves stacking order throughout device integration, the approach yields devices with a disorder mean free path exceeding 200 μm at cryogenic temperatures, as confirmed by transverse magnetic focusing measurements. Within the flat surface bands of RMG, the authors observe a size-driven crossover from Poiseuille to porous electron flow in the intermediate-temperature regime of strong electron-electron hydrodynamics. This scalable source of ultraclean RMG opens the door to systematic studies of the correlated phase diagram of rhombohedral graphene across many-layer thicknesses.

*Authors: Ludwig Holleis, Youngjoon Choi, Canxun Zhang, Jack H. Farrell, Gabriel Bargas, Audrey Hsu et al.*

### Intrinsic i-wave altermagnetism in 2D graphene superlattices ([2604.19661](https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.19661))

Altermagnetism — a third class of magnetism distinct from ferromagnetism and antiferromagnetism, characterized by momentum-dependent spin splitting driven purely by magnetic order — has so far been confined to transition-metal compounds. This theoretical work demonstrates that graphene antidot superlattices are intrinsically unstable toward an altermagnetic ground state, and establishes a symmetry-guided design principle to engineer i-wave altermagnets in these carbon nanostructures. Using tight-binding and mean-field calculations, the authors show that the magnetic instability of graphene antidot lattices generates i-wave altermagnetic spin splitting without any transition-metal element, making these the first proposed metal-free 2D altermagnets. The results extend the material scope of altermagnetism and offer a pathway to carbon-based spintronic devices compatible with conventional semiconductor processing. The findings also suggest that a wide family of graphene nanostructures with appropriate symmetry may host unconventional magnetic order.

*Authors: Cuiju Yu, Jose L. Lado (Aalto University, Finland)*

### Nickel intercalation in epitaxial graphene on SiC(0001): a novel platform for engineering two-dimensional heterostructures ([2604.21739](https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.21739))

Two-dimensional magnetic materials integrated with graphene are highly sought after for next-generation spintronics, but nickel in its 2D form has been largely inaccessible due to synthesis challenges. This work demonstrates controlled intercalation of Ni beneath epitaxial graphene on the Si-face of SiC(0001) via a scalable colloidal nanoparticle route: chemically synthesized Ni nanoparticles (~10 nm) are deposited on graphene by room-temperature immersion in colloidal solution, and subsequent thermal annealing at 650 °C drives intercalation, yielding well-ordered Ni islands at the graphene/buffer-layer interface. Scanning tunneling microscopy, angle-resolved photoemission spectroscopy, and density functional theory calculations elucidate the atomic structure and electronic properties of the intercalated Ni layers. The resulting graphene/Ni/SiC heterostructure constitutes a novel 2D spintronic platform where graphene's electronic properties are modulated by proximity to the underlying 2D magnetic layer. The colloidal nanoparticle approach is scalable and applicable to other metal intercalants, broadening the scope of 2D heterostructure engineering on SiC.

*Authors: Y. Vlamidis et al.*

### Radio-Frequency-Driven Reshaping of the Mesoscale Charge-Density-Wave Landscape in 1T-TaS₂ Thin-Film Devices ([2604.00463](https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.00463))

Charge-density-wave (CDW) materials such as quasi-2D 1T-TaS₂ exhibit rich domain textures whose control is critical for proposed CDW-based memory and logic applications. This work demonstrates that radio-frequency (RF) excitation directly reshapes the mesoscale CDW landscape in 1T-TaS₂ thin-film devices: under combined RF and DC bias, the hysteretic current-voltage characteristics associated with the nearly commensurate–incommensurate CDW transition display RF-driven collapse, branching, and multiple step-like features that depend on frequency and drive amplitude. In-situ Raman spectroscopy under RF drive reveals enhanced intensity and linewidth narrowing of low-frequency CDW phonon modes, consistent with reduced dephasing and increased coherence of the periodic lattice distortion — signatures of annealed CDW domain configurations. Simulations confirm that oscillatory driving reduces domain-wall density and reorganizes the discommensuration network by annealing frustrated configurations. These results establish RF engineering as an effective tool for controlling CDW mesoscale order and tuning device characteristics in 2D CDW thin-film systems.

*Note: RF frequency range (MHz) is adjacent to the microwave band and represents the same class of high-frequency electromagnetic manipulation of condensed-matter devices.*
