## Extended Summaries: 2D Materials & Microwave Engineering

### Phase-selective orbital-charge conversion in MoTe₂ — [2607.01623](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.01623)
Molybdenum ditelluride (MoTe₂) is a van der Waals transition metal dichalcogenide (TMD) that exhibits a unique thickness-driven structural phase transition, making it an ideal platform for studying orbital transport phenomena in two-dimensional systems. This study uses RF sputtering to deposit MoTe₂ thin films across a range of thicknesses, discovering a critical crossover at ~4.5 nm: below this thickness the films adopt the metallic monoclinic 1T' phase, while thicker films stabilize in the semiconducting hexagonal 2H phase. Raman spectroscopy and scanning tunneling spectroscopy confirm the structural and electronic changes, with gapless behavior in the ultrathin 1T' films and a finite bandgap in 2H films. Spin-pumping ferromagnetic resonance experiments reveal a transverse charge-conversion signal—indicative of orbital Hall transport—exclusively in the metallic 1T' phase, which is absent in the 2H phase. These results establish a phase-selective control mechanism for orbitronic devices, where the same material's orbital-to-charge conversion efficiency can be switched on/off by thickness engineering, with direct implications for energy-efficient spintronics and next-generation TMD-based devices.

### Anisotropic nanoscale coherent polariton transport in CrSBr — [2607.02071](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.02071)
CrSBr is an air-stable van der Waals antiferromagnetic semiconductor with exceptionally strong excitonic resonances, making it a leading platform for studying exciton-polariton physics in 2D magnetic materials. In this combined experimental-theoretical study, cathodoluminescence (CL) nanoscopy — exploiting the short wavelength of the exciting electron beam — is used to map polariton propagation with nanometric spatial resolution in thin CrSBr slabs, entirely without external optical cavities. The key finding is that self-hybridized polaritons (formed by coupling of ultra-high oscillator strength excitons with the slab's own photonic modes) exhibit strongly anisotropic transport: coherent polariton propagation is confined exclusively to the crystallographic a-axis in accordance with the material's C₂ᵥ symmetry. This directionality arises from the intrinsic optical anisotropy of CrSBr's layered crystal structure and has direct consequences for designing polaritonic waveguides and on-chip optical interconnects based on 2D magnetic materials. The work opens new avenues for magnetically tunable, symmetry-protected polariton routing in van der Waals systems.

### Interfacial Strain and Structural Defects Govern the Performance of Tantalum Superconducting Waveguide Resonators — [2607.02238](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.02238)
Tantalum is currently considered among the best available materials for superconducting microwave resonators and transmon qubits, due to its low surface oxide loss tangent and high kinetic inductance ratio; however, the microscopic origins of quality factor variation between fabrication runs remain poorly understood. This work systematically investigates 200 nm thick Ta films sputter-deposited on high-resistivity Si(100) substrates across a substrate temperature range of 20–600°C and on three different seed layers (Nb, TiN, TaN), combining structural characterization (XRD, TEM) with microwave resonator measurements at 100 mK in the single-photon regime. The critical finding is that the α-Ta phase (body-centered cubic, low-loss) is reliably stabilized above 500°C or on any of the tested metallic seed layers, with internal quality factors reaching up to 1.5 million — competitive with state-of-the-art results. Structural analysis reveals that interfacial strain and grain-boundary defects, controlled by deposition temperature and seed layer choice, are the primary loss mechanisms limiting resonator performance. These results provide actionable fabrication guidelines for producing high-Q Ta microwave resonators for superconducting quantum processors and quantum sensing applications.

### Disorder-induced superconductivity in graphene — [2607.02267](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.02267)
Monolayer graphene's semi-metallic band structure with a vanishing density of states at the Dirac point normally requires extremely strong electron-electron interactions to achieve a superconducting instability, placing intrinsic graphene far from conventional Cooper pairing conditions. This theoretical study by van Poppelen, Löthman, and Black-Schaffer demonstrates a conceptually distinct route: introducing dilute concentrations of vacancies or hydrogen adsorbates generates a large density of low-energy quasi-localized states (resonant impurity states) that effectively renormalizes graphene toward a finite density of states, dramatically lowering the pairing threshold. Using self-consistent mean-field calculations, the authors show that even moderate disorder concentrations (~1%) are sufficient to nucleate superconducting order in monolayer graphene, without requiring twisted-bilayer alignment or proximity effects. The mechanism is analogous to the disorder-enhanced superconductivity known in amorphous Al and Bi films, but here applied to a 2D Dirac material where disorder acts constructively rather than destructively. This work reframes impurity engineering as a viable strategy for achieving superconductivity in pristine 2D semimetals and may explain unconventional pairing reported in chemically modified graphene systems.

### Ising superconductivity and anomalous metallic states in a bulk crystal with artificial unidirectional stacking layers — [2607.01302](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.01302)
Ising superconductivity — characterized by strong in-plane upper critical fields far exceeding the Pauli limit due to spin-momentum locking — has until now been confined to genuinely atomically thin TMD monolayers or few-layer flakes, where inversion symmetry is broken by reduced dimensionality. This work introduces Sr₀.₇₅ClNbS₂, a bulk crystal in which planar Sr-Cl spacer networks force all NbS₂ layers into unidirectional (AA) parallel stacking, thereby breaking interlayer inversion symmetry throughout the bulk and creating a macroscopic Ising superconductor. Magnetotransport measurements confirm a large in-plane upper critical field enhancement characteristic of Ising pairing, while also uncovering a previously unreported magnetic-field-induced anomalous metallic state where resistivity saturates at a finite value yet the Hall response vanishes — a signature inconsistent with both normal and fully superconducting behavior. The approach of using intercalation chemistry to engineer stacking order provides a scalable route to extending monolayer-like electronic phases into bulk, ambient-stable single crystals. These results have broad implications for integrating Ising superconductors into conventional device architectures without the handling challenges of ultrathin 2D flakes.
