## Extended Summaries: 2D Materials & Microwave Engineering

### Magnon polaritons in a van der Waals ferromagnet coupled to a superconducting resonator — [2605.12298](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.12298)

This paper by Alvaro Bermejillo-Seco and collaborators achieves a long-sought milestone in 2D quantum magnonics: strong magnon-photon hybridization in a two-dimensional van der Waals ferromagnet, Cr₂Ge₂Te₆ (CGT), a canonical 2D Ising ferromagnet with a bulk Curie temperature of ~61 K. The authors overcome the challenge of small spin volumes in 2D magnets by engineering a low-impedance superconducting microwave resonator that concentrates the microwave magnetic field in the region of the exfoliated CGT flakes, achieving sufficiently strong coupling despite flake thicknesses as low as 30 nm. Across six independently fabricated devices, reproducible avoided crossings in the microwave transmission spectrum confirm hybridization between the resonator photon mode and the ferromagnetic resonance (FMR) of the CGT flake, constituting genuine magnon polaritons. The measured vacuum coupling rate scales with the square root of flake thickness as expected theoretically, and extrapolation predicts that magnon-photon hybridization in the monolayer limit is achievable with the current resonator design—opening a direct route to quantum-coherent experiments with individual 2D magnetic layers integrated into superconducting microwave circuits.

### Probing charge noise in bilayer graphene quantum dots by Landau-Zener-Stückelberg-Majorana spectroscopy — [2605.12257](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.12257)

This work from Katrin Hecker, S. Möller, L. Banszerus, and co-workers at RWTH Aachen and Forschungszentrum Jülich addresses a critical bottleneck for bilayer graphene (BLG) as a spin-qubit host: the unexplored charge noise environment at microwave frequencies. The authors form a charge qubit in a BLG double quantum dot and drive it through the interdot charge transition repeatedly at GHz frequencies (5–10 GHz) using Landau-Zener–Stückelberg–Majorana (LZSM) interference—a technique that encodes the noise spectral density in the visibility and periodicity of interference fringes. From the LZSM patterns, they extract a noise spectral density of order 0.5–0.9 neV/√Hz, which is comparable to GaAs and Si/SiGe qubits, establishing that charge noise in BLG does not constitute a fundamental barrier to coherent spin-qubit operation. The microwave-frequency sensitivity of LZSM spectroscopy provides access to the regime most relevant for qubit gate speeds, and the results benchmark BLG alongside the most established semiconductor qubit platforms.

### Multilayer model for coatings with arbitrary layers for superconducting radio-frequency applications — [2605.07958](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.07958)

Aaron Gobeyn, Wolfgang Ackermann, and Herbert De Gersem (TU Darmstadt) present a significant extension of the theoretical toolkit for designing superconducting radio-frequency (SRF) accelerator cavities coated with multilayer thin-film stacks. The established SIS tri-layer framework is generalized to handle an arbitrary sequence of superconducting, normal-conducting, and insulating layers of varying thicknesses, accounting for all ohmic and dielectric loss channels within a unified Ginzburg–Landau–London framework. A key finding is that the optimal structure for maximizing the applied magnetic field before vortex penetration remains the single SIS (n=1) case, but that the thickness of each superconducting coating layer can be reduced significantly below its London penetration depth with only minor performance degradation. This framework directly enables quantitative optimization of SRF cavity coatings involving emerging materials (NbN, MgB₂, iron-based superconductors) for next-generation particle accelerators where improvements in Q₀ and accelerating gradient are urgently needed.

### Breaking the Trade-off: Bulk 2D Ising Superconductivity with High Tc and Giant Interlayer Spacing via Chain Intercalation in (BaS)₁/₃TaS₂ — [2605.07336](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.07336)

Ziyi Zhu and co-workers report the synthesis and characterization of (BaS)₁/₃TaS₂, which resolves a long-standing materials challenge in 2D transition metal dichalcogenide superconductors: conventional intercalation consistently reduces Tc when increasing interlayer spacing to decouple TaS₂ layers and maximize 2D Ising character. The authors circumvent this by intercalating Ba-S-S-Ba chains between TaS₂ bilayers rather than between individual layers, tripling the inter-bilayer spacing to 12.75 Å relative to pristine 2H-TaS₂ while simultaneously breaking bulk c-axis mirror symmetry within each TaS₂ bilayer (a prerequisite for in-plane Zeeman-type spin-orbit locking and the 2D Ising pairing mechanism). The compound exhibits a robust out-of-plane upper critical field well exceeding the Pauli paramagnetic limit, consistent with protected spin-triplet-like Ising pairing, and is the first bulk crystal to simultaneously exhibit high Tc and giant interlayer spacings. This establishes chain intercalation as a new design principle for engineering quantum materials where dimensional crossover and superconducting pairing strength must be independently tuned.

### Signatures of time-reversal-symmetry breaking in multiband 2H-TaS₂ revealed by zero-field Josephson nonreciprocity — [2605.00477](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.00477)

Daniel Margineda and collaborators report on a van der Waals Josephson junction geometry providing transport-level evidence for a spontaneous time-reversal symmetry (TRS) breaking superconducting state in 2H-TaS₂, a known 2D Ising superconductor. The zero-field Josephson diode effect observed in 2H-TaS₂/2H-NbSe₂ junctions—a nonreciprocal critical current that cannot be induced by external magnetic fields or geometric asymmetry alone—provides a direct transport signature of TRS breaking in the bulk order parameter. Complementary observation of a nonlinear Hall effect further corroborates simultaneous breaking of time-reversal and inversion symmetry in the multiband superconducting state. The results are significant because TRS breaking in an apparently conventional spin-singlet TMD superconductor is unexpected from single-band BCS theory, and the multiband scenario proposed—where different bands develop superconducting phases with a relative phase difference—would place 2H-TaS₂ in an unconventional class alongside UTe₂ and Sr₂RuO₄.
