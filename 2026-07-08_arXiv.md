## Extended Summaries: 2D Materials & Microwave Engineering

### Anisotropic nanoscale coherent polariton transport in CrSBr — [2607.02071](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.02071)
*(2D material — van der Waals antiferromagnet)*

Malik, Ko, Solovyeva et al. report a combined experimental and theoretical study of polariton propagation in CrSBr, a layered van der Waals antiferromagnet with exceptionally large exciton oscillator strength. The experiment employs cathodoluminescence (CL) spectroscopy, which provides nanometer spatial resolution defined by the electron beam wavelength and is thus well suited to probing polaritons in thin slabs without the need for external cavity mirrors. The polaritons form via self-hybridization between the bound excitons and a thin-slab photonic mode confined within the CrSBr crystal itself. The resulting polariton propagation is strongly anisotropic — reflecting the crystal's in-plane optical anisotropy and the directional nature of the slab photonic mode — and can be tracked coherently at the nanoscale. This work establishes CrSBr as a platform for nanoscale polaritonic waveguiding in 2D magnets, with implications for spin-optical devices and magnon-polariton information processing.

### Anisotropic tunneling through magnetic barriers in 8-Pmmn borophene — [2607.02077](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.02077)
*(2D material — monolayer borophene)*

El Aitouni, Zriouel, Cortes, Laroze, and Jellal study electron tunneling across magnetic barriers in 8-Pmmn borophene, a 2D allotrope of boron notable for its tilted, anisotropic Dirac cones distinct from those in graphene. The magnetic barriers are modeled by two ferromagnetic strips deposited atop the borophene sheet. Using the low-energy anisotropic Dirac Hamiltonian, the authors solve for spinor wavefunctions in three regions and impose continuity conditions to extract transmission and reflection as functions of incident energy, angle, and barrier strength. The key finding is that the tilt of the Dirac cone introduces a strong angular anisotropy in transmission, with complete suppression for specific incident directions and resonance-like peaks in others. This directional filtering effect — absent in isotropic Dirac materials — suggests that 8-Pmmn borophene magnetic-barrier junctions can function as momentum-selective valves for electron transport, with possible applications in 2D electronics and spintronics.

### First principles study of chalcogen vacancy effect on the optoelectronic and photocatalytic properties of transition metal dichalcogenides monolayers — [2607.02776](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.02776)
*(2D material — TMD monolayers: MoS₂, WS₂, MoSe₂, WSe₂)*

This DFT study systematically examines how chalcogen (S or Se) vacancies alter the electronic and photocatalytic properties of the four prototypical MX₂ monolayers. Structural relaxation shows that M–X bond lengths near a vacancy deviate from pristine values, and band structure calculations reveal that vacancies introduce in-gap states near the Fermi level, reducing the effective band gap. Crucially, electrostatic potential calculations demonstrate that vacancies break the mirror symmetry perpendicular to the monolayer plane, inducing an asymmetry in the electrostatic potential on opposite faces of the sheet. This built-in electric field drives spatial charge separation under illumination — a feature entirely absent in pristine monolayers. The work predicts that controlled chalcogen vacancy engineering is a viable route to activate monolayer TMDs as photocatalysts for water splitting and CO₂ reduction, pointing toward intentional defect engineering as a design strategy for 2D photocatalytic devices.

### Disorder-induced superconductivity in graphene — [2607.02267](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.02267)
*(2D material — monolayer graphene)*

Van Poppelen, Löthman, and Black-Schaffer demonstrate theoretically that disorder — specifically vacancies or hydrogenated carbon sites — can trigger superconductivity in monolayer graphene even for arbitrarily weak pairing interactions. In the clean limit, graphene's semi-metallic Dirac spectrum has a vanishing density of states at the charge-neutrality point, requiring unrealistically strong interactions to pair. Introducing low concentrations of lattice defects generates a large density of quasi-localized low-energy (near-zero) states reminiscent of the mid-gap states known from the Haldane-Anderson impurity physics of bipartite lattices; these states provide the spectral weight needed for Cooper pair formation at weak coupling. For conventional s-wave pairing, the authors find a finite superconducting order parameter as soon as disorder is present, while the gap structure and real-space distribution of the condensate reflect the defect-induced localization. The result suggests that the anomalous metallic states sometimes observed in gated graphene devices could have a superconducting origin, and that defect engineering (e.g. via hydrogenation patterning) is a handle on inducing and tuning superconductivity in graphene without twisting or gating.

### Ising superconductivity and anomalous metallic states in a bulk crystal with artificial unidirectional stacking layers — [2607.01302](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.01302)
*(2D material — NbS₂ layers in bulk)*

Liu, Xu, Wang et al. report the synthesis of Sr₀.₇₅ClNbS₂, a bulk crystal in which a planar Sr–Cl network enforces complete reorganization of the NbS₂ stacking from the native antiparallel AB configuration into a parallel AA arrangement — a combination never previously realized in a macroscopic crystal. The AA stacking breaks inversion symmetry along the c-axis while preserving it within each NbS₂ layer, thereby activating the Ising spin-orbit coupling intrinsic to transition metal dichalcogenide monolayers across a centimeter-scale single crystal. Transport measurements reveal Ising superconductivity with anomalously large in-plane upper critical field well above the Pauli paramagnetic limit, as well as anomalous metallic states above Tc consistent with quantum phase fluctuations in a two-dimensional superconductor. This work demonstrates that deliberately engineered stacking sequences can impart monolayer-like quantum phenomena to macroscopic bulk crystals, providing a scalable route to Ising and topological superconductivity without the constraints of mechanical exfoliation.

### Imaging superconducting weak spots through vortex-assisted THz near-field photovoltage — [2607.00914](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.00914)
*(Microwave/THz engineering — superconducting circuit diagnostics)*

Salvía-Fernández, Khestanova, Velluire-Pellat et al. report the first implementation of THz near-field photovoltage nanoscopy on a current-biased superconducting strip, achieving 300 nm spatial resolution at 2.52 THz in a NbN film. The technique exploits the sensitivity of vortex dynamics to local superfluid density: at nanoscopic defects where the condensate is suppressed, THz illumination preferentially nucleates vortex-antivortex pairs, generating detectable photovoltage signals that map the defect landscape. Scanning the near-field tip across the NbN strip reveals isolated peaks within the bulk that directly correlate with sub-resolution inhomogeneities too small to be detected by conventional optical or electron microscopy. Because NbN and related nitride films are the material backbone of superconducting qubits and kinetic inductance detectors operating in the microwave GHz range, identifying and localizing sub-gap defects at nanometer scale is directly relevant to reducing two-level system noise and improving microwave resonator quality factors — limitations that currently constrain coherence times in state-of-the-art superconducting quantum processors.
