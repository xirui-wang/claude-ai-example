## Extended Summaries: 2D Materials & Microwave Engineering

### [Anomalous magneto-optical response at RuO₂/WSe₂ van der Waals interface](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.20262)
This work investigates interfacial magnetism at (001)-RuO₂ films using monolayer WSe₂ as a sensitive magnetic proximity probe in a vdW heterostructure. Magneto-optical Kerr effect measurements reveal an anomalous response that tracks the field-induced polarization of interfacial magnetic moments at the RuO₂ surface rather than the bulk altermagnet order. The sensitivity of WSe₂ to proximity-induced spin splitting makes it a powerful surface magnetometry tool, opening a route to electrically tunable valleytronics driven by altermagnetic proximity. These results demonstrate that 2D TMDs can directly probe otherwise inaccessible surface magnetic phases in oxide heterostructures, which is significant for both spintronics and the emerging field of altermagnetic materials.

### [Van der Waals engineering of valley polarization in WSe₂ via kagome V₂O₃ monolayer heterostructure through magnetic proximity effect](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.15666)
This first-principles study establishes a 2D ferromagnetic oxide (kagome V₂O₃ monolayer) as a magnetic proximity partner for WSe₂, finding that the intrinsic ferromagnetism of V₂O₃ induces a spontaneous valley splitting of ~10.41 meV in WSe₂ via strong interfacial exchange coupling and charge redistribution mediated by V 3d electrons. This value significantly exceeds splittings achievable by external magnetic fields, making the system attractive for valley-polarized devices at zero field. The oxide-driven valleytronic platform requires no external field, leverages the large spin-orbit coupling of WSe₂, and could in principle be electrically gated via the V₂O₃ magnetic state. The work proposes a generalizable strategy for engineering valley polarization in TMD monolayers using 2D magnetic oxide proximity layers.

### [Controllable Quantum Spin Hall Phases in Bi₂Te₃-Family van der Waals Heterobilayers](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.20541)
This study demonstrates that stacking two individually-trivial quintuple layers from the Bi₂Te₃ family into a vdW heterobilayer generates a non-trivial quantum spin Hall phase through interfacial band hybridization. First-principles calculations show the QSH edge states are tunable by interlayer strain and external electric fields, enabling on/off switching of topological transport—a key requirement for topological field-effect transistors. Crucially, the QSH edge channels remain robust against interlayer twist, indicating resilience to fabrication imperfections common in vdW assembly. These results suggest that "topology-by-proximity" stacking of trivial Bi₂Te₃-family materials offers a practical and tunable route to 2D topological devices without requiring intrinsically topological constituents.

### [Charge imprinting biases topology of correlated insulator in hBN-aligned rhombohedral multilayer graphene](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.20377)
This theoretical work studies correlated Chern insulators in rhombohedral multilayer graphene (RMG) aligned with hexagonal boron nitride (hBN), finding that the topology of the insulating ground state at integer filling is strongly biased by a "charge imprinting" mechanism from the hBN moiré potential. Hartree-Fock calculations show that the hBN's moiré potential shapes the valence-band charge texture near the interface, which then acts as a long-range Coulomb template imprinting a preferred Chern number on the doped conduction electrons far from the interface. Correlated insulators are most robust at small twist angles and intermediate layer numbers (N ≈ 6), where bandwidth suppression and wavefunction delocalization are balanced. This work reveals a non-local electrostatic mechanism—charge imprinting—by which a proximate substrate can deterministically select the topological character of a correlated insulator in a 2D moiré system.

### [Cavity Enhanced Superconductivity](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.19171)
This experimental work demonstrates for the first time that vacuum electromagnetic fluctuations in a resonant cavity can *enhance* rather than suppress superconductivity. By carefully tuning a complementary split-ring resonator to the 2.04 THz Raman-active phonon mode of few-layer NbSe₂, the authors observe a ~10% increase in Tc (3.02 K → 3.41 K) in trilayer NbSe₂. The enhancement tracks the spatial profile of the cavity field and shows a non-monotonic frequency dependence, ruling out trivial heating effects and pointing to genuine phonon-mediated cavity backaction on Cooper pairing. First-principles QEDFT calculations by co-authors support a mechanism in which cavity-modified phonon spectral weight reinforces the electron-phonon coupling. This result establishes THz resonator engineering as a new knob for enhancing superconductivity in layered quantum materials, with direct implications for 2D superconductor-based quantum devices.

### [Ultra-high Q-factor superconducting tantalum resonators on 300 mm Si wafers](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.10719)
This work reports α-Ta superconducting microwave coplanar-waveguide resonators fabricated using industrial processes on 300 mm ultra-high-resistivity (> 10 kΩ·cm) intrinsic silicon wafers, achieving median internal quality factors exceeding 40 million and maxima above 60 million—among the highest reported for planar superconducting resonators. Energy-participation-ratio analysis identifies a dominant participation-controlled interface loss mechanism and places a conservative upper bound on substrate-associated dissipation, with the best substrates yielding an inferred loss tangent below 1.0 × 10⁻⁸. Demonstrating scalable industrial compatibility on 300 mm substrates is a critical step toward manufacturable superconducting quantum processors, as it removes the gate of wafer-scale fabrication being limited to specialized lab-grown materials. These results establish α-Ta on MCZ silicon as a leading platform for next-generation low-loss superconducting quantum circuits operating in the microwave regime.
