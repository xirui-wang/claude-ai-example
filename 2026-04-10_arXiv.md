## Extended Summaries: 2D Materials & Microwave Engineering

### Physics-Informed 3D Atomic Reconstruction and Dynamics of Free-Standing Graphene from Single Low-Dose TEM Images
**[2604.07271](https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.07271)** — Xiaojun Zhang, Shih-Wei Hung, Yawei Wu, Jyh-Pin Chou, Angus I. Kirkland, Roar Kilaas, Fu-Rong Chen

Understanding the three-dimensional rippling of free-standing graphene is essential because the intrinsic out-of-plane corrugations govern local strain fields and hence the electronic band structure of this quintessential 2D material. However, conventional atomic-resolution TEM requires electron doses that cause radiation damage before a single reconstruction can be completed. This work introduces a physics-informed computational framework that recovers 3D atomic coordinates from a single TEM frame acquired at only 8×10³ e⁻/Å² with 1 ms temporal resolution, combining simulated annealing optimization with molecular dynamics regularization as a physical prior. The method achieves sub-ångström out-of-plane accuracy, enabling real-time tracking of graphene ripple dynamics at time scales relevant to phonon fluctuations. These results open a new paradigm for studying 2D materials under minimally invasive conditions and could be extended to other radiation-sensitive van der Waals monolayers.

### Determining the Free-Carrier Fraction in 2D Perovskites using Power Dependent Photoluminescence
**[2604.07262](https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.07262)** — Antonella Cutrupi, Marc Melendez Schofield, Raquel Utrera-Melero, Michel Frising, Enrique Arevalo Rodriguez, Upasana Das, Ferry Prins

Two-dimensional Ruddlesden-Popper perovskites host excitonic states whose binding energies span a wide range depending on the number of inorganic layers n, making them model systems for probing the exciton-to-free-carrier transition as a function of quantum confinement. The authors present a complete analytical framework that directly extracts the free-carrier fraction from power-dependent peak photoluminescence intensities using the Saha equation as the governing thermodynamic relationship. By systematically varying n and examining the crossover from tightly bound excitons (n=1) through the mixed regime, the method provides a single-measurement diagnostic for the optical excited-state nature without time-resolved spectroscopy. This quantitative handle is critical for device design: photovoltaics require maximizing free-carrier populations, while LEDs benefit from tightly bound excitons with high radiative efficiency. The framework generalizes to other nanostructured semiconductor systems with tunable confinement.

### Microscopic evidence of spin-driven multiferroicity and topological spin textures in monolayer NiI₂
**[2604.06959](https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.06959)**

Monolayer NiI₂ is a rare example of a 2D multiferroic, displaying coexisting magnetic and electric orders even at the single-layer limit, making it a high-priority target for fundamental studies of magnetoelectric coupling. Using vectorial spin-polarized scanning tunneling microscopy, the authors fully determine the spin rotation plane of a canted spin-spiral ground state and reveal an accompanying 2Q charge modulation that tracks the spiral periodicity. Crucially, spin spiral domain walls host topological spin textures identified as meron/antimeron pairs—half-skyrmion-like objects carrying fractional topological charge—which represent a new class of 2D topological defects in a van der Waals material. The results provide microscopic evidence that multiferroicity in monolayer NiI₂ is spin-driven (type-II mechanism) and rule out competing explanations invoking multi-component ferro-axial order. The meron/antimeron pairs at domain walls offer a platform for low-dissipation, electric-field-controlled topological spin-texture manipulation in ultrathin devices.

### Light-controlled van der Waals tunnel junctions: mechanisms, architectures, functionalities, and opportunities
**[2604.08399](https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.08399)** — Mohamed Shehabeldin (Boston College), Pablo Jarillo-Herrero (MIT) et al.

Van der Waals heterostructures assembled from atomically thin 2D layers provide a uniquely clean platform for tunneling spectroscopy and devices because dangling-bond-free interfaces preserve the intrinsic electronic structure of each layer. This invited review systematically covers how light modifies charge transport across such junctions through three distinct mechanisms: photogating (persistent doping via trapped photogenerated carriers), photovoltaic effects (built-in field-driven carrier separation), and intrinsic photoconductive modulation of tunnel transmission. Emerging functionalities surveyed include photodetection approaching quantum noise limits, electrically driven light emission, optical memory via persistent photoconductance, and tunneling-driven sensing of moiré and layer-specific quantum geometry. The authors also chart forward-looking opportunities in twist-resolved optical spectroscopy of moiré flat bands, moiré ferroelectric tunnel junctions as non-volatile photomemory, and scalable architectures integrating photoresponsive tunnel junctions into computing and sensing systems. This work maps a comprehensive design space for next-generation optically reconfigurable quantum devices built from 2D materials.

### Detection of spin- and valley-polarized states in van der Waals materials via thermoelectric and non-reciprocal transport
**[2604.02427](https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.02427)** — Oladunjoye A. Awoga, Pauli Virtanen, Tero T. Heikkilä, Stefan Ilić

Valley polarization—the imbalanced population of inequivalent K and K' valleys—is a hallmark of broken inversion or time-reversal symmetry in transition metal dichalcogenide monolayers and moiré graphene systems, but direct experimental probes beyond optical techniques remain scarce. This work predicts that thermoelectric and current-rectification (non-reciprocal) transport signatures emerge in hybrid junctions combining an Ising superconductor with a few-layer TMD or twisted/rhombohedral graphene hosting valley-polarized states, arising from the interplay of intrinsic Ising spin-orbit coupling, spin-splitting from an exchange or Zeeman field, and valley polarization. Both effects are analytically linked to the valley degree of freedom, providing experimentally accessible transport fingerprints that do not require optical excitation. The predicted signals are large enough to be detectable with standard cryogenic transport setups, offering a practical route to characterize correlated and superconducting states with valley structure in van der Waals heterostructures.

### Strongly Correlated Superconductivity in Twisted Bilayer Graphene: A Gutzwiller Study
**[2604.04631](https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.04631)** — Matthew Shu Liang et al.

Magic-angle twisted bilayer graphene (MATBG) harbors superconductivity in a regime where the kinetic energy is quenched by the moiré flat bands and correlation effects are paramount, yet the pairing mechanism remains debated. This study employs a Gutzwiller variational wavefunction applied to an 8-band model of MATBG (correlated f-orbitals + uncorrelated c-orbitals), uniquely allowing the Gutzwiller projector to break charge U(1) symmetry to admit superconducting order. At filling ν=−2.5, the resulting phase diagram reveals a dome-shaped Fermi liquid region separating weakly correlated BCS-like SC at small Hubbard U from a strongly correlated SC at large U. In the realistic parameter regime of MATBG, a nematic SC state is stabilized over a wide area of parameter space; at large U, interaction-driven gap reconstruction produces a nodal gap structure with V-shaped quasiparticle density of states. These findings provide a non-perturbative benchmark connecting the strongly correlated normal state to the superconducting order parameter topology in 2D moiré systems.
