## Extended Summaries: 2D Materials & Microwave Engineering

### ⭐ [2606.06152] 2.4 GHz Flip-flop Device within Nonequilibrium Superconducting Diode (2D Materials + Microwave Engineering)
This paper engineers thickness-controlled inversion-symmetry breaking in few-layer flakes of the van der Waals superconductor 2M-WS₂ to create a nonequilibrium Josephson diode with 67% rectification efficiency and an on/off ratio exceeding 10⁵. Under microwave excitation at 2.4 GHz, the device performs edge-triggered flip-flop logic—the first demonstration of GHz-frequency digital logic within a 2D van der Waals superconducting device. The operational frequency spans 12 orders of magnitude, pointing to broad applicability from DC to microwave regimes. The device's combination of a 2D material platform with microwave-frequency switching makes it directly relevant to cryogenic microwave signal routing in quantum information processing architectures. The nonequilibrium Josephson junction mechanism, rather than equilibrium anomalous supercurrent, provides a new design degree of freedom for 2D superconducting logic elements.

### ⭐ [2606.05356] Fermiology and the Candidate Chiral Superconductor in Rhombohedral Tetralayer Graphene (2D Materials)
Rhombohedral tetralayer graphene is a moiré-free van der Waals 2D system whose superconducting phase, unlike twisted bilayer graphene, is free of periodic potential engineering, making its pairing mechanism intrinsic to the rhombohedral stacking topology. This work uses quantum oscillation measurements to directly map the Fermi surface within the superconducting dome, finding that the normal state spontaneously breaks time-reversal symmetry—an essential prerequisite for chiral p+ip-wave superconductivity. The coincidence of the time-reversal broken metal and the superconducting region in the phase diagram strongly suggests that chiral pairing, rather than conventional singlet pairing, develops in this 2D graphene multilayer. This represents a landmark step toward identifying a topological superconductor in a 2D carbon system, with potential applications in Majorana-based quantum computing. The results establish rhombohedral multilayer graphene as a key platform for tunable topological superconductivity without proximity engineering.

### ⭐ [2606.04728] Curvature-driven revival of charge density waves in non-Euclidean space (2D Materials)
This work constructs a monolayer TiSe₂-NbSe₂ van der Waals heterostructure on a BLG/SiC substrate with engineered non-Euclidean geometric deformations (nanoscale curvature). Low-temperature ARPES reveals that in the flat region, massive interfacial charge transfer destroys Fermi surface nesting and suppresses the TiSe₂ CDW—a conventional tuning effect—yet in the curved region, the local geometric strain revives CDW order, revealing curvature as a thermodynamic control knob for 2D electronic ordered phases. The demonstration that non-Euclidean geometry can counteract extreme electronic perturbations to stabilize quantum order opens a new class of structural control over emergent phenomena in 2D heterostructures. This approach extends the concept of strain engineering in 2D materials to the topologically nontrivial regime where local curvature modifies effective electron-phonon coupling. The findings suggest that flexible or substrate-textured 2D device architectures can use curvature as a tunable axis for phase control.

### ⭐ [2606.04953] Electron-Phonon Coupling and CDW Instabilities in W₂N and Halogen-Functionalized W₂N Monolayers (2D Materials)
Pristine monolayer W₂N is predicted by first-principles calculations to host phonon softening at both the M and K high-symmetry points arising from strong electron-phonon coupling, making it both a charge density wave and a strong-coupling superconductor with Tc=13.2 K and λ=1.00. Fluorination of the surface transforms W₂NF₂ into a moderate-coupling superconductor (Tc=5.3 K, λ=0.67) by selectively suppressing the soft-phonon anomaly, while chlorination has intermediate effects. This demonstrates that surface functionalization provides chemical tunability of the CDW-superconductor competition in 2D transition-metal nitride monolayers—a class less explored than TMDs—without changing the crystal structure. The tunability of Tc and the CDW instability within the same structural family makes W₂N monolayers a promising platform for studying CDW-superconductor coexistence and competition in 2D.

### ⭐ [2606.04954] Triangular CDW (T-CDW) Stabilization in Janus Group-VI Chalcogenide Hydrides (2D Materials)
Janus 2D materials—where the two surfaces present different chalcogen species—break out-of-plane mirror symmetry and generate intrinsic electric dipole fields absent in symmetric TMDs. In 1T-WSH and 1T-WSeH, first-principles calculations show pronounced M-point phonon softening in the high-symmetry 1T structure, driving a 2×2 CDW reconstruction with a distinctive triangular pattern (T-CDW). Crucially, this T-CDW distortion acts as a self-regulating mechanism: it reduces excessive electron-phonon coupling that would otherwise destroy superconductivity, allowing both CDW and superconducting orders to coexist at low temperatures. The triangular CDW topology differs from the conventional star-of-David CDW in 1T-TaS₂ and 1T-TiSe₂, pointing to a new class of CDW symmetry in Janus monolayers. These results expand the palette of competing orders available in Janus 2D materials and suggest that hydrogenation is a powerful surface chemistry route to CDW engineering.

### ⭐ [2606.03663] Enhanced Superconductivity in Atomically Thin Noble Metals (2D Materials)
Although Au, Ag, and Cu are conventionally non-superconducting in bulk, quantum confinement in atomically thin films dramatically enhances the density of states near the Fermi level and softens acoustic phonon branches, yielding intrinsic superconductivity: Tc≈0.78 K in trilayer Cu and Tc≈0.63 K in pentalayer Au by first-principles Eliashberg calculations. When Cu films are grown on h-BN/Cu(111) heterostructures, an interface-induced Lifshitz transition shifts the Fermi level through a van Hove singularity, and the resulting Tc critically depends on the precise interfacial stacking configuration (AA vs. AB). This establishes a general design principle linking quantum confinement, phonon softening, and interfacial symmetry breaking to engineer superconductivity in ultra-thin films of otherwise non-superconducting metals. The work highlights noble metal thin films on 2D substrates as an unexplored platform for proximity-free 2D superconductivity, with potential for gate-tunable Tc via electrostatic control of the Lifshitz transition.

### ⭐ [2606.06490] Coherent Room-Temperature Dipole Synchronization in Nanocavity Sheets (2D Materials)
Plasmonic nanocavity sheets consist of 2D arrays of nanometer-gap metallic cavities that confine optical fields to sub-nm volumes, enabling near-field dipole coupling over nanoscale distances within a planar geometry. Under non-resonant CW pumping, this work demonstrates that the array develops a synchronized dipole state characterized by spatial coherence across the array but with suppressed temporal photon coherence—a phase distinct from both lasing and photonic Bose-Einstein condensation. The synchronization mechanism relies on near-field cavity-mediated interaction rather than long-range photon exchange, and operates at room temperature without cryogenic infrastructure. This finding opens a pathway to room-temperature quantum optical coherence in 2D plasmonic architectures, with potential applications in collective quantum emitter control, nanoscale sensing, and coherent light generation at ambient conditions.

### ⭐ [2606.06465] Superconducting Triode Effect in a Quantum-Dot Josephson Junction with Biased Top Gate (Microwave Engineering)
This proposal introduces a superconducting triode—a three-terminal device with continuously gate-tunable non-reciprocal supercurrent—based on an asymmetric quantum-dot Josephson junction coupled to a metallic top gate that breaks parity symmetry while preserving time-reversal. Under microwave-frequency RF irradiation, the junction produces highly asymmetric Shapiro steps, where quantized voltage plateaus appear predominantly for one bias polarity, implementing microwave-controlled quantized supercurrent rectification. This Shapiro-step asymmetry serves as a direct microwave probe of superconducting non-reciprocity and a mechanism for phase-coherent microwave rectification. The all-electrical gate control of diode polarity and efficiency makes this architecture attractive for programmable microwave superconducting circuits, including in quantum-information hardware where microwave-controlled unidirectional current flow is a key primitive.

### ⭐ [2606.06069] Forbidden Transitions in Superconducting Artificial Atoms (Microwave Engineering)
Superconducting qubits are driven by microwave photons primarily through electric dipole transitions, with higher-order (quadrupole, magnetic dipole) transitions typically invisible under uniform-field excitation due to selection rules. This work shows that by accounting for the full spatial and vectorial profile of the microwave electromagnetic field over the Josephson junction, quadrupole transitions can be excited precisely at locations where the electric field is zero—directly violating the standard dipole selection rule through structured light. The effect enables access to transitions otherwise forbidden in conventional microwave drives, adding new spectroscopic lines and control channels to superconducting artificial atoms. This structured-field control is relevant to frequency-multiplexed qubit addressing and to quantum error correction schemes requiring access to higher levels of artificial atoms without increasing drive power. The finding motivates the development of on-chip microwave field-shaping structures for next-generation superconducting quantum processors.
