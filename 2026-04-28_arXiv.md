## Extended Summaries: 2D Materials & Microwave Engineering

### [Cryogenic shock exfoliation for ultrahigh mobility rhombohedral graphite nanoelectronics](https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.21912)
**arXiv:2604.21912** | Ludwig Holleis, Youngjoon Choi, Canxun Zhang, Jack H. Farrell, Gabriel Bargas, Audrey Hsu, Zexing Chen, Ian Sackin, Wenjie Zhou, Yi Guo, Thibault Charpentier, Yifan Jiang, Benjamin A. Foutty, Aidan Keough, Martin E. Huber, Takashi Taniguchi, Kenji Watanabe, Andrew Lucas, Andrea F. Young

Rhombohedral multilayer graphene (RMG) has recently emerged as a uniquely tunable 2D platform for gate-controlled correlated electron phases — including superconductors, magnets, and topological states — but progress has been severely limited by the rarity of rhombohedral stacking in natural graphite, which occurs predominantly in small flakes unsuitable for scalable device fabrication. This work introduces cryogenic shock exfoliation, in which graphite crystals are rapidly quenched to liquid-nitrogen temperatures and mechanically cleaved under cryogenic conditions, causing preferential fracture along rhombohedral stacking boundaries and dramatically increasing the yield and area of rhombohedral flakes. Combined with low-pressure van der Waals assembly, the method produces encapsulated RMG devices exceeding 1300 μm² with 90% fabrication yield. Crucially, the resulting devices exhibit disorder mean free paths exceeding 200 μm at low temperature — comparable to the best-ever reported for graphene — and spatially uniform spin magnetism mapped over a 10×10 μm² area, confirming that the technique does not introduce additional disorder. These results remove a central bottleneck in RMG research and should accelerate the controlled study of its rich correlated phase diagram, including the recently discovered spontaneous superconductivity and quantum anomalous Hall phases, at large device scales.

### [Dynamic Moiré Potentials and Robust Wigner Crystallization in Large-Scale Twisted Transition Metal Dichalcogenides](https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.22343)
**arXiv:2604.22343** | Yifan Ke, Chuanjing Zeng, Xinming Qin, Wei-Lin Tu, Wei Hu, Jinglong Yang

Understanding the collective behavior of electrons in twisted van der Waals bilayers requires bridging ab initio accuracy and the experimental scale — a challenge particularly severe in transition metal dichalcogenide (TMD) moiré systems, where moiré unit cells contain thousands of atoms and lattice relaxation critically reshapes the electronic landscape. This work develops a machine-learning workflow that couples the DeePMD force field and the DeepH Hamiltonian framework, both trained on DFT data, to simulate the full structural and electronic dynamics of twisted bilayer WS₂ with over 3000 atoms at experimentally relevant twist angles. The key finding is that low-temperature lattice vibrations and relaxation cooperate to deepen moiré potential wells, narrow the lowest conduction band, and concentrate electronic spectral weight into strongly localized Wannier-like states — effects absent in frozen-lattice models. Using DFT-derived moiré potentials that incorporate these dynamical effects as input to density-matrix renormalization group (DMRG) many-body simulations, the authors demonstrate robust Wigner crystallization and a kagomé-patterned three-electron charge-ordered state, in quantitative agreement with recent optical and transport measurements in TMD heterobilayers. The study establishes a scalable ML-enabled pipeline for realistic simulation of correlated physics in moiré 2D materials, with direct implications for designing TMD platforms for quantum simulation of frustrated lattice models.
