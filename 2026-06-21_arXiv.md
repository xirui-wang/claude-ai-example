## Extended Summaries: 2D Materials & Microwave Engineering

### Anomalous magneto-optical response at RuO₂/WSe₂ van der Waals interface ([2606.20262](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.20262))

Shaikh et al. place a monolayer of WSe₂—a 2D transition-metal dichalcogenide with well-resolved excitonic resonances—directly atop (001)-oriented RuO₂ thin films to probe the putative altermagnetic ground state of RuO₂ via the magnetic proximity effect. Temperature-dependent magneto-optical Kerr/reflectance spectroscopy reveals an anomalous excitonic energy shift and a deviation from the conventional Varshni temperature dependence below 55 K that is absent in a reference WSe₂ sample encapsulated in hBN without RuO₂. Crucially, the sign of the anomalous shift reverses upon field-cooling with opposite magnetic polarity, unambiguously demonstrating a magnetic origin and providing one of the first optical signatures of surface magnetism in RuO₂. The use of monolayer WSe₂ as a proximity sensor is particularly significant because it is highly sensitive to interfacial exchange fields and eliminates bulk magnetic contributions. These results advance the understanding of altermagnetic RuO₂ and establish a 2D-material-based spectroscopic platform for studying weak interfacial magnetism in unconventional magnetic candidates.

### Chemical Vapor Deposition of Ni-doped Iron Germanium Telluride Nanosheets ([2606.18546](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.18546))

Metcalf et al. report the first CVD synthesis of Ni-doped FGT (FemGenTe₂) nanosheets on SiO₂/Si substrates, an important advance because CVD offers scalability and semiconductor-industry compatibility not shared by mechanical exfoliation or flux growth. Undoped and Ni-doped samples with multiple Fe concentrations and a fixed 4% Ni-to-Fe ratio were produced, and the resulting nanosheets are characterized structurally to confirm the layered van der Waals structure. Ni doping in FGT is known to modulate the Curie temperature and magnetic moment, making the dopant concentration a lever for tuning the balance between itinerant ferromagnetism and Kondo-lattice behavior. The CMOS-compatible process potentially enables integration of 2D magnetic layers into device stacks for spintronics and proximity-effect experiments. This work fills a critical gap in the synthesis toolkit for magnetic van der Waals materials.

### Charge imprinting biases topology of correlated insulator in hBN-aligned rhombohedral multilayer graphene ([2606.20377](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.20377))

Qiao et al. use self-consistent Hartree-Fock calculations to study how hexagonal boron nitride (hBN) alignment affects correlated Chern insulating phases in rhombohedral multilayer graphene at small twist angles. They find that even when the active carriers are displaced many layers away from the moiré interface, the hBN moiré potential imprints a non-trivial charge texture onto the valence bands near the interface—a phenomenon they term "charge imprinting." This charge imprinting acts as an effective sublattice potential that biases the spontaneous symmetry-breaking in the correlated state, strongly favoring certain Chern numbers over others. The mechanism explains experimental observations in which hBN-aligned rhombohedral tetralayer and pentalayer graphene samples consistently exhibit Chern insulators with a particular sign, whereas unaligned samples show both signs with comparable frequency. These results highlight the non-local nature of moiré potential effects in graphene heterostructures and have direct implications for engineering topological phases in 2D material stacks.

### Emergence of Resonating Valence-Bond Correlations in Stretched Graphene ([2606.18973](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.18973))

Azadi et al. investigate how uniaxial tensile strain applied to graphene alters its electronic correlation strength using variational Monte Carlo (VMC) and diffusion Monte Carlo (DMC) benchmarked against conventional Jastrow-Slater wave functions. By comparing a standard single-determinant ansatz with a resonating-valence-bond (RVB) Jastrow-antisymmetrized geminal product, they quantify the energy gain due to RVB correlations as a function of bond expansion. Strikingly, the RVB energy gain first increases with strain up to a critical lattice expansion and then decreases, tracing a non-monotonic curve that signals an evolution from a weakly correlated metal toward a strongly correlated phase and then a partial reversal as the system enters a different regime. This finding implies that moderate tensile strain—achievable experimentally by substrate engineering or suspended membrane geometry—can substantially enhance correlation effects in a material otherwise considered weakly correlated. The work has broader significance for understanding correlation-driven instabilities (including potential superconductivity) in strained graphene and related 2D honeycomb lattices.
