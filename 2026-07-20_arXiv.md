## Extended Summaries: 2D Materials & Microwave Engineering

### [2607.14712] Direct observation of anisotropic exciton dispersion in the 2D semiconductor CrSBr
**Authors:** Yiwen Song, Peiyi He, Weizhe Zhang, Wenyuan Ouyang, Wenjing Liu, Jinlong Du, Zuxin Chen, Jiuyu Sun, Peng Gao, Yu Ye (Peking University)

CrSBr is an emerging layered 2D magnetic semiconductor that hosts tightly bound excitons strongly coupled to its magnetic order, making it a model system for probing light–matter interactions in van der Waals magnets. This work reports the first momentum-resolved measurement of exciton dispersion in multilayer CrSBr using defocus-engineered electron energy-loss spectroscopy (EELS), supported by first-principles calculations. The key finding is a pronounced in-plane anisotropy: the lowest-energy exciton shows a linear dispersion with a slope of 7.02 eV Å along the ΓY direction—among the largest values reported in any low-dimensional material—while remaining nearly dispersionless along ΓX. This remarkable anisotropy reflects the strongly anisotropic crystal structure and modified Rytova–Keldysh screening in the 2D limit. These results directly constrain theoretical models of exciton transport in CrSBr and provide a quantitative benchmark for designing spintronic and excitonic devices that exploit the anisotropic optical response of 2D magnetic semiconductors.

### [2607.15722] Drift-Induced Nonreciprocal Hyperbolic Polaritons in Graphene/α-MoO₃ Heterostructures
**Authors:** Rajveer Fandan, Jorge Pedrós

Hyperbolic phonon-polaritons in α-MoO₃ provide deeply sub-diffractional, directional light confinement in the mid-infrared, but conventional heterostructure designs lack active tunability. This work investigates the effect of drift currents in graphene—an electrically tunable 2D Dirac semimetal—on hyperbolic polariton propagation in graphene/α-MoO₃ van der Waals heterostructures. Drift currents break effective time-reversal symmetry in the 2D electron system, inducing nonreciprocal polariton propagation in which wavevectors along and against the drift direction are inequivalent. The authors demonstrate that this nonreciprocity can be actively switched and tuned via gate voltage and drift current density, without changing the dielectric environment. This demonstrates a new mechanism for electrically reconfigurable nonreciprocal nanophotonics entirely within the 2D material platform, with potential applications in on-chip isolators, circulators, and active infrared nano-antennas.

### [2607.10049] Engineering Topology by Design in Two-dimensional Materials
**Authors:** Arjyama Bordoloi, Sobhit Singh

The initial materials landscape for 2D topological insulators (2D TIs) was limited to systems with sufficiently large intrinsic spin–orbit coupling to drive band inversion, placing demanding constraints on material choice. This review describes a paradigm shift toward engineering topological phases by design in van der Waals (vdW) heterostructures: stacking different 2D layers creates interfacial coupling that can induce nontrivial band topology even when neither constituent is intrinsically topological. Multiple experimental handles—twist angle, stacking order, electric displacement field, and chemical functionalization—provide rich control over the topological phase diagram. Proximity-induced SOC from heavy-element 2D layers can drive band inversion in otherwise trivial graphene or other light-element 2D materials, while symmetry breaking at the interface further enriches the possible topological phases. The authors discuss concrete material platforms (graphene/TMD, TMD/TMD heterostructures), recent experimental realizations of interface-engineered topological states, and outstanding challenges in predicting and characterizing proximity-induced topology, arguing that this interface-engineering framework greatly broadens the accessible materials landscape for next-generation topological devices.

### [2607.15009] Ridge-Spin-Layer Coupling and Emergent Ridgetronics in 2D Altermagnets
**Authors:** Mu Tian, Run-Wu Zhang, Chaoxi Cui, Zhi-Ming Yu, Yugui Yao et al.

Valleytronics exploits isolated points in momentum space as binary information carriers, but this work introduces a fundamentally new paradigm based on one-dimensional ridges—continuous flat lines of dispersionless electronic states in reciprocal space—in two-dimensional altermagnets. The core mechanism, ridge-spin-layer coupling (RSLC), locks the ridge orientation in k-space simultaneously to the electron spin and to the atomic sublayer index, so that switching ridge orientation selects a particular spin channel and sublayer in real space. This coupling enables (i) layer-selective reversal of ridge orientation via external fields, (ii) spin-filtered charge transport without the need for spin–orbit coupling, and (iii) a distinctive electric Hall response distinct from anomalous Hall effects. Using collinear spin layer group symmetry analysis, the authors predict three 2D candidate materials—Mg₂Mo₂(PO₅)₂, Ca(FeP)₂, and Mg₂V₂(SO₅)₂—with the requisite symmetry to host RSLC. This "ridgetronics" platform extends flat-band physics to a new controllable degree of freedom and opens a direction for spin-selective device design in 2D magnetic materials without spin–orbit coupling.

### [2607.13834] Wireless millikelvin interconnects for superconducting quantum hardware
**Authors:** Kristopher Barr, Mingyan Zhong, Euan Parry, Manoj Stanley, Qusay Al-Taai, Paniz Foshat, Kaveh Delfanazari, Martin Weides, Nick M. Ridler, Chong Li, Alessandro Rossi (University of Strathclyde and collaborators)

A central scalability bottleneck in superconducting quantum computing is the dense bundle of coaxial cables linking millikelvin-temperature quantum processors to room-temperature electronics: each cable introduces thermal power into the dilution refrigerator, limiting the number of qubits per fridge. This paper demonstrates a wireless alternative by showing that a superconducting microwave resonator—of the type routinely used for dispersive qubit readout at ~4–8 GHz—can be coherently driven via electromagnetic coupling across the cryostat walls, operating entirely at millikelvin temperatures without any direct electrical feed-through. The wireless link is characterized in terms of coupling efficiency, residual heat load, and resonator quality factor. This proof-of-concept establishes that wireless microwave interconnects can operate in the demanding low-temperature, low-power regime of superconducting quantum hardware, and positions the approach as a complement to cryogenic CMOS and microwave-to-optical transduction strategies for reducing wiring complexity and enabling larger-scale quantum processors.

### [2607.13870] Phonon down-conversion by normal metals for superconducting devices
**Authors:** Guglielmo La Magna, Gianluigi Catelani (Forschungszentrum Jülich)

Quasiparticle poisoning—the breaking of Cooper pairs by pair-breaking phonons with energies above 2Δ—is a key source of energy relaxation and correlated errors in superconducting microwave quantum circuits, including transmon qubits and kinetic inductance photon detectors. High-energy phonons are generated by cosmic rays, radioactive decay events, and substrate acoustic phonon absorption, and can propagate to superconducting devices before decaying. This work develops a kinetic-equation model for phonon down-conversion by a normal-metal thin film deposited on the superconductor: electron–phonon scattering in the metal reduces the phonon energy below the pair-breaking threshold 2Δ, neutralizing the threat before phonons reach the superconducting circuit element. The authors derive both analytical and numerical steady-state solutions for the down-conversion efficiency as a function of electron–phonon coupling strength and the phonon transmission coefficient at the normal-metal/superconductor interface. This framework provides quantitative design guidance for incorporating normal-metal phonon absorbers into superconducting microwave device layouts, directly relevant to protecting qubit coherence in fault-tolerant quantum processors operating in realistic radiation environments.
