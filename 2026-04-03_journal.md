## Extended Summaries: 2D Materials & Microwave Engineering

### Super-moiré spin textures in twisted two-dimensional antiferromagnets
*(Nature Nanotechnology, K. C. Wong, R. Peng, E. Anderson et al.)*

Twisted van der Waals antiferromagnets offer a new platform for engineering magnetic textures by combining moiré superlattice engineering with magnetic order. In this work, the authors employed scanning nitrogen-vacancy (NV) magnetometry to image the magnetic stray fields of twisted double-bilayer chromium triiodide (CrI₃) devices at small twist angles. They discovered long-range spin textures that extend well beyond the single moiré unit cell — reaching up to ~300 nm in 1.1° devices, an order of magnitude larger than the underlying moiré wavelength — and dubbed these "super-moiré magnetic states." The magnetic field maps are consistent with the formation of antiferromagnetic Néel-type skyrmions that span multiple moiré cells, a geometry qualitatively distinct from conventional moiré magnetic textures. These results demonstrate that twist angle is a powerful knob for generating and tuning emergent topological spin textures in 2D magnets, with potential implications for ultra-dense magnetic data storage and spintronic devices exploiting topological protection.

### Bandwidth-tuned Mott transition and superconductivity in moiré WSe₂
*(Nature, Y. Xia, Z. Han, K. F. Mak, J. Shan et al.)*

The origin of high-temperature superconductivity in strongly correlated materials — particularly cuprates — is one of the defining open problems in condensed matter physics, and the Hubbard model is widely believed to capture its essential physics. Moiré WSe₂ provides a uniquely tunable solid-state realisation of the Hubbard model where bandwidth, filling, and interaction strength can each be independently controlled by twist angle and electrostatic gating. The authors mapped the full phase diagram of moiré WSe₂ as a function of carrier density and observe an antiferromagnetic Mott insulating state at half filling (ν = 1), superconducting domes on both the electron- and hole-doped sides, and an anomalous strange-metallic state at elevated temperatures with linear-in-T resistivity. The peak superconducting Tc occurs immediately adjacent to the Mott transition, reaching approximately 6% of the effective Fermi temperature — comparable to optimal cuprate superconductors in the same metric — suggesting that proximity to the Mott-insulating state is a key ingredient for high-Tc superconductivity. This work establishes moiré WSe₂ as a clean, tunable analogue of the cuprate phase diagram and provides direct experimental access to the bandwidth-tuned Mott transition at low disorder.

### Angle evolution of the superconducting phase diagram in twisted bilayer WSe₂
*(Nature, Y. Guo, C. R. Dean et al.)*

Twisted bilayer WSe₂ has emerged as a prominent moiré superconductor, but prior reports at 3.65° and 5.0° had produced seemingly distinct phase diagrams, raising questions about whether the two devices share a common superconducting mechanism. This work systematically fabricated and characterised devices across the full range of twist angles between these limits and compared experimental results to angle-dependent theoretical predictions. The superconducting state is found to evolve smoothly and continuously with twist angle, ruling out a qualitative change in mechanism. At all twist angles, superconductivity is proximal to a Fermi surface reconstruction associated with antiferromagnetic ordering, yet is neither tied to the van Hove singularity nor to the half-band Mott insulator. These findings unify the existing phenomenology of WSe₂ moiré superconductivity, establish it as a robust feature of the triangular Hubbard model at moderate filling, and highlight the power of moiré 2D material platforms for systematically mapping the interplay between correlation-driven instabilities and unconventional superconductivity.

### Reentrant superconductivity in a naturally occurring Josephson junction array tuned by radio-frequency power
*(Nature Communications, S. Avraham, S. Sankar, S. Sandik et al.)*

Granular aluminium is a disordered superconductor in which aluminium grains embedded in an aluminium oxide matrix form a naturally occurring array of Josephson junctions, making it an ideal platform for studying collective quantum effects in Josephson arrays without the need for lithographic fabrication. The authors demonstrate that applying radio-frequency (RF) power to granular aluminium films induces a striking reentrant superconducting transition as a function of both temperature and magnetic field: samples that are resistive at base temperature become superconducting upon increasing the RF drive power. At low temperatures and sufficient RF drive, giant Shapiro steps emerge with an amplitude consistent with coherent phase locking across the full junction array, amplifying the quantized voltage far beyond what a single junction would produce. The reentrant behavior is attributed to RF-induced many-body screening of electronic charge disorder — a correlation effect that restores phase coherence across the array. These results demonstrate microwave-frequency control of macroscopic quantum phase coherence in a naturally disordered superconductor, with potential implications for RF-tunable quantum circuit elements and cryogenic microwave signal processing.
