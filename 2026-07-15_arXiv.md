## Extended Summaries: 2D Materials & Microwave Engineering

### [2607.10049] Engineering Topology by Design in Two-dimensional Materials
*Arjyama Bordoloi, Sobhit Singh — submitted 11 Jul 2026*

Two-dimensional topological insulators (2D TIs) host dissipationless edge states protected by time-reversal symmetry and are prime targets for spintronic devices. This paper reviews a paradigm shift from intrinsic-SOC-driven band inversion to an interface-engineering approach in van der Waals (vdW) heterostructures, where nontrivial topology emerges from interlayer coupling rather than from bulk material properties alone. The framework exposes multiple independent tuning knobs—stacking geometry, twist angle, and chemical functionalization—that collectively allow systematic control over the topological gap and the number of topological edge modes. By decoupling the topological design from the intrinsic chemistry of any single layer, this strategy dramatically expands the accessible materials space for realizing 2D TIs at or above room temperature.

### [2607.08926] Interface-induced spin-resolved type-II band alignment and enhanced magnetic anisotropy in MSe₂/WTe₂ van der Waals heterobilayers
*Paras Poswal, Ranjit Pati, Neeraj Shukla*

Van der Waals heterobilayers of transition-metal dichalcogenides (TMDs) offer a unique platform for 2D spintronics, as the interface can dramatically alter spin-dependent electronic structure relative to the individual layers. Here, first-principles calculations reveal that the energetically preferred AA'-stacked WSe₂/WTe₂ heterobilayer exhibits type-II band alignment with a gap of 0.70 eV. Replacing the W atoms with magnetic 3d metals (V, Cr, Mn, Fe, Co) induces long-range ferromagnetic order and reconstructs the spin-resolved band structure, while the heterointerface generates a pronounced built-in electric field via charge redistribution that enhances perpendicular magnetic anisotropy compared to the individual TMD layers. These results suggest that TMD-heterostructure engineering is a viable route to achieving gate-tunable spin textures and magneto-anisotropy for next-generation 2D spintronic devices.

### [2607.11721] High-field Josephson effect enabled by a moiré Hofstadter spectrum
*A. Díez-Carlón, M. Cárdenes Wuttig, N. Wei, D. Ivanov, P. Altpeter, P. Hakonen, K. Watanabe, T. Taniguchi, L. I. Glazman, D. K. Efetov — submitted 13 Jul 2026*

Magnetic fields ordinarily suppress Josephson transport by dephasing Cooper pairs, limiting coherent junctions to low fields in conventional materials. This work shows that moiré engineering of graphene Josephson junctions enables phase-coherent Josephson interference up to 6 T—well into the fractal Hofstadter-butterfly regime—by exploiting Landau-level reconstruction within the moiré superlattice to maintain phase coherence at high flux per unit cell. The results are explained by a theoretical model in which the Hofstadter spectrum creates unusually robust current-carrying channels that survive to fields far exceeding those accessible in single-layer or AB-stacked graphene junctions. This demonstration opens opportunities for high-field superconducting quantum devices based on 2D moiré materials.

### [2607.00809] Vanadium superconducting microwave resonators on silicon wafers
*Y. Fujita, Y. Urade, Y. Hibino, M. Tsujimoto, K. Inomata, G. Fujii, W. Mizubayashi (AIST, Japan) — published Phys. Rev. B 114, 024201 (2026)*

Superconducting microwave resonators are foundational components of quantum information processors and microwave photon detectors, and materials optimization is critical for minimizing internal loss. This work demonstrates vanadium thin-film coplanar waveguide resonators fabricated on silicon substrates, characterizing their microwave quality factors as a function of temperature, power, and film processing conditions. The study finds that vanadium's relatively high critical temperature (~5 K) and compatibility with silicon processing makes it an attractive alternative to aluminum or niobium for hybrid quantum circuits. The results are compared with standard dielectric-loss models, and pathways for further improving Q-factors through surface treatment and substrate engineering are identified, with direct relevance to quantum computing and single-photon microwave detection applications.
