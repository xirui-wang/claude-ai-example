## Extended Summaries: 2D Materials & Microwave Engineering

### [2607.03841] First-principles study of the electronic structure and optical properties of two-dimensional α-graphdiyne
Ashkan Shekaari investigates monolayer α-graphdiyne (α-GDY) using density-functional theory within the plane-wave pseudopotential framework. The electronic band structure reveals a gapless Dirac crossing at the K point of the hexagonal Brillouin zone, establishing α-GDY as a two-dimensional Dirac semimetal—a class of materials with massless charge carriers analogous to graphene but with a distinct acetylenic-linkage-based carbon network. The density of states analysis confirms that electronic states near the Fermi level are dominated by carbon 2p orbitals, with minimal 2s contribution. The optical response is markedly polarization-dependent: the in-plane dielectric function exhibits a Drude-like response and negative real values at low photon energies, indicating high optical conductivity, while the out-of-plane response is comparatively flat. These findings position α-GDY as a potential platform for plasmonic, photovoltaic, and optoelectronic applications within the growing 2D carbon allotrope family.

### [2607.02267] Disorder-induced superconductivity in graphene
Jannes van Poppelen, Tomas Löthman, and Annica M. Black-Schaffer demonstrate that superconductivity can emerge in monolayer graphene purely as a consequence of structural disorder, without requiring strong electron–electron interactions. In clean graphene the semi-metallic linear dispersion yields a vanishing density of states at the Dirac point, making conventional Cooper pairing prohibitively difficult. The authors show that introducing a low concentration of point defects—vacancies or chemisorbed hydrogen atoms—creates a large density of low-energy impurity states near the Fermi level that dramatically lower the threshold for s-wave pairing with even arbitrarily weak attractive interactions. Crucially, global phase coherence is not destroyed by the inhomogeneous disorder landscape; instead, it is sustained through a finite superfluid weight of purely geometrical (Berry-phase) origin rather than kinetic energy. For unconventional nearest-neighbor pairing, the system exhibits quantum-critical-like behavior yet retains long-range phase coherence, with mixed d-wave symmetries. This work suggests that controlled defect engineering could be a practical route to inducing superconductivity in graphene devices.

### [2607.06547] Half state at ν_tot = −1/2 and its transition in Decoupled Twisted Double Bilayer Graphene
Ning Ma, Kenji Watanabe, Takashi Taniguchi, and Mitali Banerjee report an experimental study of a correlated half-filling state in decoupled twisted double bilayer graphene (tDBLG), a platform consisting of two van der Waals stacked graphene bilayers with a controlled interlayer twist angle. The team identifies a robust correlated state at total filling ν_tot = −1/2 and maps its phase transitions as a function of gate voltage, displacement field, and magnetic field. The decoupled geometry of the device allows the two bilayer subsystems to be studied as individual but coupled correlated electron systems, providing clean access to the half-state physics without complications from strong interlayer hybridization. The phase transitions observed at ν_tot = −1/2 reveal competing ordered phases and offer a new window into the rich many-body phase diagram of moiré graphene systems, with implications for understanding fractionalization and Wigner crystal formation in two-dimensional electron systems.

### [2607.06520] Multi-Knob Switchable Chiral Superconductivity Quartet in Rhombohedral Graphene
Zhenqi Hua, Shenyong Ye, Phatthanon Pattanakanvijit, Gang Shi, Tonghang Han, Emily Aitken, and collaborators report the identification of a quartet of distinct chiral superconducting states in rhombohedral multilayer graphene that can be switched between using multiple in-situ control parameters. Building on prior observations of unconventional superconductivity in pentalayer and hexalayer rhombohedral graphene systems, this work demonstrates that the pairing symmetry and chirality can be toggled by tuning electrostatic gating, magnetic field, and displacement field independently. The four-fold multiplicity of superconducting states is argued to arise from the interplay of spin-valley degeneracy and time-reversal symmetry breaking, producing a rich order-parameter landscape in the rhombohedral graphene phase diagram. The ability to switch chirality with multiple independent knobs is significant for applications in topological quantum computing, where chiral superconductors are predicted to host chiral Majorana edge modes.

### [2607.06390] Effect of charge-imbalance potential relaxation on the high-frequency vortex dynamics and kinetic inductance of superconducting circuits
This theoretical paper addresses a fundamental mechanism affecting the microwave performance of type-II superconducting devices operating in the vortex state. The authors derive the equation of motion for an Abrikosov vortex starting from the time-dependent Ginzburg-Landau (TDGL) equations, explicitly incorporating the relaxation of the charge-imbalance (branch-imbalance) electrochemical potential—a quantity describing the non-equilibrium imbalance between electron-like and hole-like quasiparticles near a vortex core. The charge-imbalance relaxation introduces retardation effects into the vortex dynamics, which the authors show manifest as a non-trivial frequency dependence of the kinetic inductance and loss tangent of superconducting resonators and transmission lines. These retardation effects are particularly pronounced at microwave frequencies (GHz range) and in materials with long charge-imbalance relaxation lengths, making them directly relevant for the design of kinetic inductance detectors (KIDs), superconducting microwave resonators for quantum computing, and other high-frequency superconducting circuits operated in magnetic fields.

### Anomalous ultrafast lithium-ion transport through boron nitride nanotube membranes [Nature Nanotechnology 21, 831–838 (2026)]
Researchers at the University of Illinois Chicago and international collaborators fabricate macroscopic membranes of vertically aligned boron nitride nanotubes (BNNTs) with nanotube densities up to 10⁷ pores cm⁻², and measure aqueous alkali-metal-ion transport through 3 nm and 12 nm diameter BNNTs. They find that Li⁺ diffusion through the BNNT channels is anomalously fast—up to 31-fold faster than Fickian diffusion predictions—and is highly selective over K⁺ and Na⁺. This transport behavior is fundamentally distinct from that in carbon nanotubes of comparable diameter and in two-dimensional boron nitride nanochannels, pointing to a unique electrostatic confinement mechanism arising from the polar B–N bond that preferentially stabilizes the smaller Li⁺ ion. The extreme selectivity and speed of Li⁺ transport demonstrated here have direct implications for energy-harvesting from salinity gradients ("blue energy") and for membrane-based lithium extraction from seawater and brines for battery manufacturing.

### Magnonic spontaneous oscillation induced by parametric pumping [Nature Communications (2026), doi:10.1038/s41467-026-71916-9]
This study demonstrates a new class of spin-wave microwave auto-oscillator using a 100-nm-thick yttrium iron garnet (YIG) stripe with two parallel coplanar waveguides (CPW) patterned on its surface as the pumping and probing elements. Under parametric microwave pumping at twice the target spin-wave frequency, spontaneous spin-wave modes are generated that exhibit quality factors exceeding 200,000—one of the highest reported for magnonic devices—and behave as auto-oscillators that can phase-lock to an injected external microwave reference signal. The spontaneous mode also functions as a magnon parametric amplifier for weak probe signals, providing gain determined by the oscillation amplitude. The results establish parametric pumping in YIG thin films as a robust and highly controllable route to nonlinear spin-wave dynamics with applications in synchronization physics, reconfigurable microwave signal processing, and magnon-based quantum information transduction.
