## Extended Summaries: 2D Materials & Microwave Engineering

### Superconductivity from dual-surface carriers in rhombohedral graphene — [Nature Physics](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41567-026-03277-5)

Kumar M. et al. report the discovery of superconductivity in rhombohedral multilayer graphene emerging from a highly unusual normal state: a charge-delocalized semimetal in which electrons reside on the top crystal surface and holes on the bottom, split across the crystal by layer polarization. This dual-surface character is made possible by the flat band topology of rhombohedral graphene, which simultaneously supports valence- and conduction-band Fermi pockets at distinct surfaces, leading to a semimetallic state rather than the usual insulating or metallic ground states. Multiple distinct superconducting phases are found as a function of carrier density and displacement field, paralleling the rich phase diagram of twisted moiré systems but arising without a moiré superlattice. The work establishes rhombohedral multilayer graphene as a clean 2D platform for studying unconventional superconductivity emerging from strongly surface-polarized carrier fluids, advancing understanding of correlated phases in flat-band graphene systems.

### Observation of propagating collective spin–valley modes in twisted WSe₂ — [Nature Physics](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41567-026-03280-w)

Xiong et al. use an ultrafast spatiotemporal imaging technique to directly visualize the propagation of charge-neutral collective excitations in twisted bilayer WSe₂ moiré superlattices near the Van Hove singularity. Two distinct propagating modes are resolved with different velocities: a fast mode consistent with a Goldstone mode of an intervalley-coherent (spin–valley superfluid) state, and a slower, likely gapped amplitude mode. The ability to image neutral spin–valley dynamics with femtosecond and nanometre resolution represents a major methodological advance for studying correlated moiré physics beyond charge transport. These results provide the first real-space evidence for collective superfluid-like behavior in the spin–valley sector of a 2D moiré material, with direct implications for topological excitations and dissipationless spin transport in van der Waals systems.

### Atomically precise synthesis and simultaneous heterostructure integration of 2D TMDs through nano-confinement — [Nature Materials](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41563-026-02495-9)

Bian C., Zhao Y., Guzman R. et al. introduce a nano-confinement chemical vapor deposition approach in which a graphene or hexagonal boron nitride capping layer is placed atop a substrate to create a confined growth environment. This confinement guides the growth kinetics of 2D transition metal dichalcogenides (TMDs), enabling atomically precise monolayer synthesis of materials including NbSe₂, MoS₂, and structurally complex Janus S–Mo–Se monolayers with both top and bottom chalcogen planes independently controlled. The method simultaneously produces ultraclean van der Waals interfaces, eliminating the polymer contamination issues associated with conventional transfer techniques. Beyond individual monolayers, the approach directly integrates TMDs into heterostructures in a single growth step, yielding morphology-controlled domains and continuous large-scale films relevant to scalable 2D device fabrication.

### Fast and Continuous Detection of Single Microwave Photons via Photoassisted Quasiparticle Tunneling — [PRL 136, 237001 (2026)](https://journals.aps.org/prl/abstract/10.1103/mj91-ntm4)

J. Basset and co-authors demonstrate a new class of superconducting single-photon detector operating in the microwave domain (10 GHz), realizing a solid-state analogue of the photoelectric effect for microwave photons. The device consists of a superconducting island connected via three Josephson junctions to a granular aluminum high-impedance microwave resonator that enhances light–matter coupling; absorption of a single photon drives a quasiparticle tunneling event that changes the island's charge parity, which is then detected continuously via microwave reflectometry. The detector achieves 10% efficiency with sub-50 ns time resolution and short dead time, enabling real-time time-resolved photon counting at the single-photon level. Applications span quantum information processing (microwave photon routing and counting), microwave quantum optics, and dark matter axion searches, making this device a key tool for emerging microwave quantum technologies.

### Interlayer exciton flux amplification driven by strong exciton confinement — [Nature Materials](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41563-026-02569-8)

Hyeongwoo Lee, Taeyoung Moon, Kyoung-Duck Park and collaborators demonstrate a novel interlayer exciton (IX) transport regime in a van der Waals heterostructure using a controllable electro-plasmonic nanocavity with sub-nanometre positional precision. By introducing a nanoscale bandgap gradient via the nanocavity, the IXs are confined to a narrow potential well, generating a sharply peaked density profile whose outward diffusion current vastly exceeds the inward electric-field-induced drift. Quantitatively, this results in an ~8,300% amplification of the diffusion current compared with conventional microscale gating—a demonstration of exciton flux engineering at the nanoscale. This work opens new possibilities for exciton-based 2D optoelectronic devices, energy-efficient light-emitting diodes, and excitonic transistors in van der Waals heterostructures, where spatial confinement provides a route to controlling excitonic fluxes far beyond conventional gating schemes.
