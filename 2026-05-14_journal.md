## Extended Summaries: 2D Materials & Microwave Engineering

### Atomically precise synthesis and simultaneous heterostructure integration of 2D TMDs through nano-confinement (Nature Materials)

This work by Bian, Zhao, Guzman et al. reports a van der Waals nano-confinement strategy in which graphene or hexagonal boron nitride capping layers are used to direct the growth kinetics of 2D transition metal dichalcogenides. The nano-confined environment enables the synthesis of NbSe2 and MoS2 monolayers with precisely tailored morphologies—from isolated domains and large-scale continuous films to intrinsically patterned ring geometries—and Janus S–Mo–Se monolayers are also achieved via van der Waals-protected bottom-plane chalcogen substitution. Critically, the approach simultaneously produces ultraclean van der Waals interfaces, bypassing the polymer contamination that plagues conventional transfer techniques. The method represents a major advance in integrating atomically precise TMD synthesis directly with heterostructure assembly, with broad implications for quantum devices based on proximity effects and moiré physics.

### Polymer-free van der Waals assembly of 2D material heterostructures using muscovite crystals (Nature Communications)

Babich, Savilov, Mamchik et al. demonstrate a polymer-free transfer method using thin muscovite (mica) crystals as the pickup medium for stacking 2D materials into van der Waals heterostructures. Temperature control over the adhesion of mica to 2D layers enables deterministic pick-up, precise alignment, and clean release without any organic polymer intermediaries, thereby eliminating the residual contamination that degrades electrical and optical properties in conventional PDMS/PC stamp methods. High-quality heterostructures produced include suspended membranes, moiré superlattices, and sensitive quantum materials that would otherwise be degraded by polymer exposure. The crystalline inorganic nature of mica ensures atomically clean interfaces, as confirmed by transmission electron microscopy, making this technique particularly significant for studying fragile correlated-electron and topological phases in moiré systems.

### Printed MoS2 memristive nanosheet networks for spiking neurons with multi-order complexity (Nature Nanotechnology)

Hadke, Klingler, Brown et al. report inkjet-printed networks of solution-processed MoS2 memristive nanosheets that exhibit thermally activated snap-back dynamics to generate physiological action-potential-like waveforms. The printed devices display multi-order temporal complexity in their spiking patterns—arising from the collective nonlinear switching dynamics of the nanosheet network—which closely mimics biological neural firing behavior. The group validated biocompatibility and functional relevance by directly stimulating mouse Purkinje neurons with the waveforms generated by these artificial neurons, and the scalability of inkjet printing enables large-area, flexible neuromorphic hardware. This work highlights MoS2 as a uniquely versatile 2D material platform for beyond-CMOS neuromorphic computing and brain–machine interface applications.

### Breakdown of the Wiedemann-Franz Law in an Interacting Quantum Hall Metamaterial (PRL)

Roche, Altimiras, Parmentier, and Maillet present a study of heat transport in a one-dimensional chain of Coulomb-blockaded metallic islands connected by ballistic quantum Hall edge channels—a microwave-compatible mesoscopic circuit architecture. In this system, charge cannot accumulate on the islands due to strong Coulomb interactions, but neutral collective excitations carry heat freely, leading to anomalous heat conductance that exceeds charge conductance and directly violates the Wiedemann–Franz law. The authors show that the Lorenz ratio scales as the square root of the chain length, rather than the universal constant expected for Fermi liquids. This work establishes a new conceptual framework for engineering heat flow in quantum Hall-based metamaterials and identifies a mesoscopic heat valve effect exploitable in cryogenic quantum circuits, directly relevant to the microwave quantum hardware community where managing heat flows at millikelvin temperatures is a key engineering challenge.

### Anyons in a Graphene Fractional Quantum Hall Interferometer (PRL)

Samuelson et al. measure anyon exchange statistics in a monolayer graphene Fabry–Pérot interferometer at Landau level filling fraction ν = 1/3. Single-event phase slips in the interference signal demonstrate quasiparticle equilibration times exceeding 20 minutes, evidencing unprecedented anyon stability in the graphene system compared to conventional GaAs-based interferometers. The graphene platform—encapsulated in hexagonal boron nitride with graphite gates—provides exceptionally clean conditions for studying fractional quantum Hall physics, and the long quasiparticle lifetimes suggest that graphene heterostructures may be superior hosts for topological quantum information protocols based on non-Abelian anyons.
