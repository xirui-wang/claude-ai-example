## Extended Summaries: 2D Materials & Microwave Engineering

### [Theory for TERS of 2D materials including out-of-plane Raman response](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.08387)
**arXiv:2605.08387** | Raul Corrêa, Luiz G. Cançado, Ado Jorio (Universidade Federal de Minas Gerais, Brazil)

Tip-Enhanced Raman Spectroscopy (TERS) provides nanoscale spatial resolution for characterizing 2D materials such as graphene and transition metal dichalcogenides (TMDs), but existing TERS theory was tailored to graphene where out-of-plane Raman response is negligible. This work derives an exact analytical expression for the electromagnetic field propagation between the metallic tip and the 2D sample, explicitly accounting for the sample-to-tip (TS) scattering pathway that becomes significant when out-of-plane phonon modes are Raman-active. The inclusion of out-of-plane response is shown to be essential for accurate TERS modeling of TMDs such as MoS₂ and WS₂, where symmetry-allowed out-of-plane modes contribute substantially to the detected signal. The framework enables quantitative extraction of local Raman tensors and field-enhancement factors from TERS measurements on arbitrary 2D materials, improving both the spatial resolution interpretation and the chemical sensitivity of the technique. This advance is significant for mapping nanoscale strain, defects, and heterostructure interfaces in next-generation 2D material devices.

### [Floquet second-order topological insulator in strained graphene](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.07190)
**arXiv:2605.07190** | (authors; Universidade Federal de Minas Gerais affiliation not confirmed)

Graphene, the archetypal 2D material, supports a rich variety of topological phases under periodic driving, and this work demonstrates a new route to Floquet higher-order topology by combining uniaxial mechanical strain with off-resonant circularly polarized light at tunable incidence angle. Uniaxial strain pushes the two Dirac cones toward a semi-Dirac (Dirac-merging) critical regime, where the Floquet-induced mass gap becomes strongly anisotropic; the additional elliptical polarization arising from oblique incidence then stabilizes a phase with gapped edges but robust in-gap corner modes in finite graphene flakes, the hallmark of a second-order topological insulator (SOTI). The mechanism is captured by a strained honeycomb tight-binding model combined with a high-frequency Floquet expansion for the effective Hamiltonian, allowing clear identification of the topological phase boundaries as a function of strain and light parameters. The results establish driven strained graphene as a minimal, experimentally accessible platform for realizing and probing Floquet SOTI physics without requiring complex van der Waals heterostructures or specialized substrates. This is particularly relevant as strain engineering in graphene is now routinely achieved through substrate patterning and tip-induced deformation techniques.
