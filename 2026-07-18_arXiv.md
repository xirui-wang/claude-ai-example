## Extended Summaries: 2D Materials & Microwave Engineering

### Chiral, Electronically Decoupled Layers of 1T'-WS2 Topological Insulator via Neutral-Molecule Intercalation ([2607.10457](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.10457))
Monolayer 1T'-WS2 is a predicted 2D topological insulator (TI), but its intrinsic electronic properties are masked by strong interlayer metallic coupling in the bulk parent phase 2M-WS2, which is also superconducting. The authors show that a simple wet-chemical amine intercalation expands the interlayer spacing from 0.5 nm to 1–4 nm, electronically decoupling the 1T' layers while preserving the intralayer framework and restructuring the stacking. The resulting insulating gap in the intercalated superlattice matches that of the isolated monolayer TI prediction, and the process is fully reversible: deintercalation restores metallic superconducting transport. This work opens direct experimental access to 2D topological edge states without requiring exfoliation to true monolayers, and establishes neutral-molecule intercalation as a versatile route for decoupling van der Waals layers across the TMD family.

### Competing Orders Driven by Wigner Crystal Phase in Rhombohedral Pentalayer Graphene ([2607.15014](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.15014))
Rhombohedral pentalayer graphene (R5G) hosts a flat band that supports strong Coulomb interactions at low carrier density and high displacement field. The authors identify a Wigner crystal (WC) insulating phase on the low-density side of the chiral superconducting dome, together with a metallic Wigner crystal (h-mWC) boundary phase near the WC perimeter. Under an out-of-plane magnetic field, field-stabilized superconductivity and unconventional reentrant quantum Hall states compete, yielding a rich magnetic-field phase diagram. These results establish R5G as a paradigmatic platform for studying the mutual reinforcement and competition between Coulomb-driven crystalline order, correlated metallicity, and topological superconductivity in a single 2D material.

### Ridge-Spin-Layer Coupling and Emergent Ridgetronics in 2D Altermagnets ([2607.15009](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.15009))
The authors introduce "ridgetronics"—a new control paradigm based on flat-band lines (ridges) in reciprocal space—demonstrating that in 2D altermagnets a continuous one-dimensional ridge locks simultaneously to spin polarization and atomic sublayer, a threefold coupling they term ridge-spin-layer coupling (RSLC). Unlike discrete valley degrees of freedom, ridges are tunable: external electric fields can switch the ridge orientation in k-space on a layer-selective basis, producing spin-filtered real-space transport and a distinct electric Hall response. First-principles calculations identify Mg₂Mo₂(PO₅)₂, Ca(FeP)₂, and Mg₂V₂ as candidate 2D altermagnet hosts of RSLC. The concept bridges flat-band physics with spin-layer control, defining a new class of spintronic functionalities exploitable in altermagnetic 2D materials.

### Gyrotropy from Extrinsic Geometry in Twisted Materials ([2607.15189](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.15189))
Gyrotropy in twisted bilayer graphene (TBG) is shown to arise entirely from the extrinsic geometric relationship between the two layers—the physical spatial offset—rather than from the intrinsic topology of the electronic Bloch states. The authors first illustrate the effect classically in a bilayer array of 1D wires, then apply the formalism to TBG, demonstrating that the gyrotropy is a quantitative measure of interlayer electronic coherence. For twisted bilayer MoTe2, the geometric gyrotropy exactly vanishes in the pristine case but becomes dominant over coherent contributions upon introducing weak strain and displacement fields. This provides a clear symmetry-based diagnostic for interlayer coupling in moiré systems, complementary to optical and transport probes.

### Superconducting Spiral Inductors for RF Reflectometry: Operation at Elevated Temperatures and Magnetic Fields ([2607.00172](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.00172))
NbTiN spiral inductors are critical components for RF reflectometry readout of semiconductor spin qubits, yet scalable quantum-computing architectures require their operation at several kelvin and fields approaching 1 T—demanding conditions poorly characterized in existing literature. The authors combine weakly coupled resonator measurements with independent two-port inductance extraction on the same device to separately attribute resonance frequency shifts and quality-factor degradation to inductive versus capacitive origins across temperature and magnetic-field sweeps. Practical design metrics are derived linking coil geometry to temperature sensitivity and magnetic-field robustness, offering a general benchmarking framework. These guidelines provide direct quantitative guidance for engineering RF-reflectometry matching networks for scalable spin-qubit readout platforms operating in realistic device environments.

### Wireless Millikelvin Interconnects for Superconducting Quantum Hardware ([2607.13834](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.13834))
A major engineering challenge for superconducting quantum processors is the dense cabling connecting millikelvin quantum chips to room-temperature control electronics, and wireless coupling at cryogenic temperatures offers a promising route to reducing wiring density and thermal load. The authors demonstrate wireless excitation of a superconducting microwave resonator—of the type used for qubit readout—operating at millikelvin inside a dilution refrigerator, directly comparing wired and wireless performance under identical cryogenic conditions. Wireless coupling faithfully reproduces the intrinsic resonator response while also exposing previously undetected parasitic electromagnetic pathways arising from stray radiation within the cryostat enclosure. This proof-of-concept establishes hardware compatibility between wireless coupling and superconducting quantum devices, and provides a diagnostic method for identifying and eliminating parasitic microwave channels in cryogenic quantum systems.

### High-Q Superconducting Microwave Resonators using MBE Titanium Nitride ([2607.15230](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.15230))
Titanium nitride (TiN) is a materials platform of choice for high-kinetic-inductance superconducting microwave resonators, but achieving consistently high quality factors requires minimizing crystalline defects that serve as microwave loss centers. The authors deposit TiN on c-plane sapphire by molecular beam epitaxy, achieving an X-ray rocking curve FWHM of 18 arcsec—the lowest reported to date—and fabricate quarter-wavelength coplanar waveguide resonators with internal Q exceeding 10⁶ at 5.8 GHz and 10 mK in the single-photon limit. Structural characterization reveals subsurface defects in the sapphire substrate that nucleate dislocations in the TiN film, identifying substrate surface preparation as a key lever for further improvement. These results set a new crystalline quality benchmark for epitaxial TiN microwave resonators and provide a clear roadmap for high-coherence superconducting qubit device fabrication.
