## Extended Summaries: 2D Materials & Microwave Engineering

### High-Q superconducting microwave resonators using MBE titanium nitride ([2607.15230](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.15230))
Ithepalli et al. employ molecular beam epitaxy to grow TiN thin films on c-plane sapphire, achieving the lowest reported X-ray rocking curve linewidth of 18 arcsec—a strong indicator of exceptional crystalline quality and a record for this material system. Despite the abrupt, atomically sharp TiN-sapphire interface confirmed by transmission electron microscopy, the study reveals for the first time that pre-existing sub-surface defects within the sapphire substrate act as nucleation sites for structural imperfections that propagate into the epitaxial TiN film. These substrate-originating defects are identified as a key performance-limiting factor for the microwave loss tangent of TiN resonators used in superconducting quantum information architectures. The finding highlights that improvements in resonator quality factors require not only optimizing TiN deposition conditions but also pre-treating or carefully selecting sapphire substrates with minimal sub-surface damage. This work provides critical practical guidance for fabricating next-generation high-impedance superconducting microwave resonators and quantum processor components based on high-kinetic-inductance TiN films.

### Contrasting Γ- and K-Valley Moiré Physics in Twisted Monolayer/Bilayer WSe₂ ([2607.18212](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.18212))
Kuklin, Mao, Shi et al. use twisted monolayer/bilayer WSe₂ as an experimental platform in which a perpendicular displacement field can continuously tune the active moiré flat band between the K-valley (d-orbital character) and Γ-valley (p-orbital character), enabling a direct orbital-controlled comparison of correlated phases. At moiré filling ν=1, the K-valley hosts a weak antiferromagnetic insulator near a van Hove singularity in the intermediate-coupling regime—similar to physics in twisted bilayer WSe₂—while the Γ-valley develops a distinct correlated state with different magnetic response. At ν=1/3, these valleys support contrasting charge-ordered states: a generalized Wigner crystal in the K-valley and a different correlated phase in the Γ-valley, reflecting the different spatial extent and interactions of d versus p orbitals in the moiré superlattice. This work establishes orbital character as an independent control parameter in 2D moiré systems, beyond filling factor and twist angle, opening new avenues for engineering exotic quantum phases. The results have broad implications for topological superconductivity and fractional Chern insulator phases in transition metal dichalcogenide moiré heterostructures.

### Valley polarization, Rashba interaction, and weak altermagnetism in MnPS₃|WS₂ van der Waals heterostructures ([2607.16454](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.16454))
Dutta, Bid, and Ganguli perform density functional theory calculations on the MnPS₃/WS₂ van der Waals heterostructure, where the antiferromagnetic MnPS₃ monolayer and the spin-orbit-coupled WS₂ monolayer combine to break inversion symmetry and generate a rich set of emergent interfacial phenomena. The calculations reveal the simultaneous emergence of an altermagnetic phase with anisotropic spin splitting in k-space, Rashba spin-orbit coupling at the interface, and spin-valley locking that ties spin polarization to the K and K' valley indices of WS₂. These phenomena arise collectively from the proximity effect between the two 2D layers and cannot be achieved in either constituent alone, demonstrating the power of vdW heterostructure engineering for spin-charge control. The Berry curvature generated by these effects is predicted to give rise to anomalous Hall-like transport signatures detectable in experiments. This MnPS₃/WS₂ system is thus identified as a highly promising candidate for low-power spintronic devices, magnetoelectric switching, and spin-to-charge conversion applications based purely on 2D materials.

### Engineering Topology by Design in Two-dimensional Materials ([2607.10049](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.10049))
Bordoloi and Singh systematically examine how van der Waals heterostructure assembly can engineer topological insulating phases in 2D materials by design, avoiding the need for intrinsic topological character in any constituent layer. The key insight is that interfacial coupling between non-topological partners can induce band inversions, proximity spin-orbit effects, and symmetry-breaking fields that collectively drive the system into a topologically non-trivial state, with stacking configuration, twist angle, and chemical functionalization as the main design handles. The review surveys examples across graphene, transition metal dichalcogenides, and magnetic 2D materials, cataloguing the phase transitions achievable through these parameters and connecting theoretical predictions to experimental realizations. A central theme is the synergy between high-throughput DFT screening and targeted epitaxial growth for efficiently discovering material combinations that host robust topological edge states. This work provides a practical design framework for 2D quantum material engineering aimed at topological quantum computing and dissipationless spin transport applications.

### Revealing the MoS₂ Growth Mechanism in CVD: Real-Time Imaging and Statistical Analysis ([2607.13893](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.13893))
Arifurrahman, Zhai, Tang et al. develop a semi-automated image processing pipeline that extracts complete crystal growth trajectories from in-situ optical microscopy videos of chemical vapor deposition of monolayer MoS₂, processing data from over 400 individual crystals in a single experimental run. Statistical analysis of the extracted growth rates, nucleation densities, and shape evolution distributions provides strong evidence that MoS₂ crystallization is governed by an edge-attachment-limited kinetic mechanism, where atoms attach preferentially at crystal edges rather than diffusing to a central terrace. This finding fundamentally distinguishes MoS₂ CVD from the diffusion-limited growth typically observed in conventional thin-film deposition, with direct implications for how process conditions such as gas flow and temperature should be optimized to control crystal size and uniformity. The high-throughput statistical approach overcomes the sampling bias inherent in conventional single-crystal tracking and enables reliable extraction of kinetic parameters not accessible from low-statistics experiments. This methodology can be directly extended to other TMD systems (WS₂, MoSe₂, WSe₂) and represents an important advance in quantitative 2D material growth science for scalable device fabrication.
