## Extended Summaries: 2D Materials & Microwave Engineering

### [Superconducting cavity probes sliding ferroelectricity in small-angle twisted WSe₂](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.22140)
*(2D Materials + Microwave Engineering — cond-mat.mes-hall primary; cross-listed cond-mat.mtrl-sci)*

Sliding ferroelectricity in parallel-stacked van der Waals bilayers — where lattice alignment induces out-of-plane electric polarization — is a recently discovered phenomenon of interest for non-volatile memory and ferroelectric FET applications. This work probes the polarization of small-angle parallel-stacked WSe₂ via the high-frequency AC response of a half-wave superconducting coplanar waveguide cavity, marking the first use of a microwave superconducting cavity as a probe of 2D ferroelectric order. Hysteretic signatures in the cavity capacitance and quality factor confirm ferroelectric switching with displacement-field cycling, while relaxation dynamics reveal domain-wall motion in the system. This non-invasive, high-frequency approach opens a new route to study ferroelectric domain dynamics in 2D materials that avoids the contact-related complications of DC transport probes.

### [Fine structure of the cyclotron resonance in heterobilayers of proximitized graphene and TMDs](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.23895)
*(2D Materials — cond-mat.mes-hall)*

Monolayer graphene acquires enhanced spin-orbit coupling when placed in proximity to a transition metal dichalcogenide (TMD) van der Waals crystal, enabling access to spin-orbit physics not native to pristine graphene. The authors analyze cyclotron resonance (CR) absorption in such graphene/TMD heterobilayers at quantizing magnetic fields, fully accounting for spin-pseudospin Rashba-type proximity coupling. Spin-textured Landau level wave functions are computed and the CR absorption spectrum is calculated, revealing: (1) a fine double-peak structure in main interband CR transitions arising from spin-split levels, and (2) additional spin-flip combined cyclotron resonance (CCR) features at distinct frequencies. These spectroscopic fingerprints provide a non-contact optical route to quantify proximity spin-orbit coupling strength in graphene heterostructures.

### [Visualizing Symmetry Broken Chern Insulators and their Quantum Melting](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.23795)
*(2D Materials — cond-mat.mes-hall; cross-listed cond-mat.str-el)*

Using scanning tunneling microscopy in aligned bilayer graphene/hBN Hofstadter systems, the authors directly visualize interaction-driven symmetry-breaking Chern insulators at fractional filling of Hofstadter bands. Measurements resolve phases with doubled, tripled, or quadrupled moiré unit cells and complex intra-unit-cell charge distributions, establishing a real-space map of correlated topological order. Two distinct quantum melting mechanisms are captured: one class of Chern states melts via appearance and proliferation of topological defects (analogous to the Kosterlitz-Thouless scenario), while others exhibit qualitatively different behavior. These results establish STM as a tool for direct real-space visualization of topological defect-driven quantum phase transitions in moiré 2D materials.

### [Electrically Programmable Correlated Topology and Magnetism in a Moiré Trilayer](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.21004)
*(2D Materials — cond-mat.str-el primary; cross-listed cond-mat.mes-hall)*

An alternating twisted trilayer MoTe₂ device provides an electrically programmable quantum many-body platform where an out-of-plane displacement field continuously modifies layer polarization, effective moiré lattice geometry, and band topology in a single sample. At zero displacement field, the triangular moiré lattice hosts a correlated insulator at ν = -1 hole per moiré unit cell. As the displacement field is increased, the effective lattice, bandwidth, and Chern numbers of the moiré bands continuously evolve, enabling in situ electrical programming of correlated topological phases and magnetism. This work represents a major step toward reconfigurable quantum materials where multiple correlated phases are accessible within one device without chemical substitution.

### [Keldysh field theory of spin- and valley-distinguished polariton nonlinearities in TMD monolayers](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.22242)
*(2D Materials — cond-mat.mes-hall)*

TMD monolayers host excitons and exciton-polaritons with nontrivial spin and valley degrees of freedom, yet existing bosonization and polariton interaction frameworks typically overlook these internal quantum numbers. This work derives a comprehensive nonequilibrium field-theory description of TMD monolayer polaritons in strong coupling using a Keldysh path-integral formalism that tracks all polariton nonlinearities — contact, exchange, and Coulomb-scattering contributions — while properly accounting for all spin and valley channels. The resulting theory provides the complete foundation for engineering photonic devices that exploit spin- and valley-selective TMD polariton nonlinearities, including optical parametric oscillators and helicity-selective switches.

### [Strongly anisotropic Rytova-Keldysh interaction and the ground state of 2D excitons](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.23033)
*(2D Materials — cond-mat.mes-hall)*

The Rytova-Keldysh potential describes the non-local, anisotropic dielectric screening of Coulomb interactions in ultrathin 2D materials and governs exciton binding energies in van der Waals semiconductors. This work derives closed-form asymptotic expressions for the anisotropic Rytova-Keldysh potential across all spatial regimes using steepest-descent integration and momentum-space coordinate scaling, revealing non-trivial amplitude scaling from the weakest-polarizability direction at intermediate distances and an anisotropic logarithmic well at short range. An anisotropic Gaussian variational ansatz then solves the Wannier exciton equation to yield an analytical formula for the anisotropic exciton ground-state binding energy in terms of the principal in-plane polarizabilities. These results provide analytically tractable starting points for exciton engineering in anisotropic 2D materials such as black phosphorus and ReS₂.

### [Probing the pairing symmetry of moiré graphene superconductors](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.23994)
*(2D Materials — cond-mat.supr-con)*

The pairing symmetry of magic-angle twisted bilayer graphene superconductors is a central unresolved question that determines the topological properties and quasiparticle structure of the condensate. This work constrains superconducting order parameters emerging from the incommensurate Kekulé spiral (IKS) normal state on the hole-doped side, finding that nodal superconductivity is the experimentally confirmed constraint but the singlet-vs-triplet and d-vector orientation remain undetermined. The authors propose definitive experimental tests based on two classes of Zeeman-field-orientation experiments to distinguish pairing symmetry: in-plane vs. out-of-plane field responses that would clearly discriminate singlet from triplet condensates. These proposals are within reach of current dilution-refrigerator vector-magnet setups used for moiré graphene measurements.

### [Displacement-Field-Driven Semimetal-Superconductor Transition in Magic-Angle Twisted Trilayer Graphene](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.20849)
*(2D Materials — cond-mat.supr-con; cross-listed cond-mat.str-el)*

Magic-angle twisted trilayer graphene (MATTG) hosts versatile correlated phases tunable by an out-of-plane displacement field D, but the theoretical description remains contested. This work argues that MATTG consists of a dispersive Dirac cone that hybridizes with the flat band from a twisted bilayer graphene (TBG) sector, with hybridization strength increasing with D; crucially, a rigid local-moment/Kondo-lattice description is invalid because the TBG Hubbard bands have a momentum-selective Mott gap that is small at the Γ point due to band topology. The D-driven semimetal-to-superconductor transition is explained within this hybridization framework, accounting for topological constraints absent in simplified Kondo descriptions. These results clarify the microscopic mechanism of displacement-field programmability in MATTG and provide a framework applicable to other moiré trilayer systems.

### [Enabling Electrical Readout of Néel Vector Reversal in a van der Waals Antiferromagnet](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.24527)
*(2D Materials — cond-mat.mtrl-sci)*

Antiferromagnetic spintronic memory devices require both writing and electrical reading of the antiferromagnetic order parameter (Néel vector), but reading the 180° Néel vector reversal has remained elusive in 2D van der Waals materials. This work reports the first experimental demonstration of electrical transport detection of complete 180° Néel vector reversal in atomically thin films of a van der Waals A-type antiferromagnet, achieved via a novel transport methodology distinct from anomalous Hall or tunneling magnetoresistance approaches. The result removes a key barrier to AFM-based 2D magnetic memory by establishing that the antiferromagnetic state can be both written and electrically read in a single atomically thin material, opening a path to scalable antiferromagnetic memory cells.

### [Cavity Enhanced Superconductivity](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.19171)
*(2D Materials + Microwave Engineering — cond-mat.supr-con)*

Vacuum electromagnetic fluctuations of confined photon modes have been predicted to modify collective quantum phases in materials, but prior experimental studies of cavity-coupled superconductors reported only suppression of Tc. This work demonstrates cavity-enhanced superconductivity in few-layer (trilayer) NbSe₂ coupled to a complementary split-ring resonator resonant at 2.04 THz, achieving approximately 10% enhancement of Tc (from 3.02 K to 3.41 K). The split-ring resonator geometry concentrates vacuum field fluctuations into the few-layer NbSe₂ film, amplifying the light-matter coupling beyond what is achievable with planar cavities. This constitutes the first experimental confirmation that vacuum electromagnetic fluctuations can enhance (rather than suppress) superconductivity, a milestone for cavity quantum materials engineering with implications for enhancing superconductivity in other 2D and thin-film systems.

### [Fractional Magnonic Frequency Combs](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.24673)
*(Microwave Engineering — cond-mat.mtrl-sci)*

Magnonic frequency combs (MFCs) — equally spaced spectral lines generated in microwave-driven high-quality magnetic spheres through parametric instabilities — are of interest for microwave signal processing applications. This work discovers fractional MFCs: when a second low-power microwave tone is applied with a precise frequency detuning relative to the comb spacing, the parametric processes compress the comb line spacings to a rational fraction of the original spacing, generating high-density spectral grids with hundreds of coherent lines. The mechanism is explained in terms of synchronization of magnonic parametric oscillators across multiple frequency scales, and conditions for achieving specific rational fractions are characterized. This discovery opens routes to high-density microwave photonic signal processing using magnonic comb generators as tunable, field-controllable spectral resources.

### [Scanning-probe quantum sensing of microwave and static magnetic field response of an on-chip superconducting resonator](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.20904)
*(Microwave Engineering — cond-mat.mes-hall; cross-listed cond-mat.supr-con)*

On-chip superconducting microwave resonators are foundational components of quantum computing processors, but their local electromagnetic field distributions and susceptibility to magnetic field-induced vortex disorder are difficult to characterize non-destructively. This work introduces scanning-probe quantum microscopy using a quantum spin sensor (e.g., NV center) to achieve nanoscale-resolution sensing of both microwave and static magnetic fields from a niobium on-chip superconducting resonator. The resonator microwave fields are used to achieve coherent spin control of the quantum sensor, while static field scans reveal the formation, spatial evolution, and pinning-depinning of superconducting vortices under applied magnetic bias with nanoscale resolution. This platform establishes a new paradigm for in situ, non-invasive characterization of superconducting quantum circuits and opens the door to local noise spectroscopy for qubit coherence optimization.
