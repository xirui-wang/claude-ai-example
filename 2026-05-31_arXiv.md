## Extended Summaries: 2D Materials & Microwave Engineering

### Visualizing orbital magnetism in electron doped rhombohedral multilayer graphene ([2605.30316](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.30316))

This work directly images the orbital magnetic texture of electron-doped rhombohedral multilayer graphene using nanoSQUID-on-tip magnetometry, covering device thicknesses from 3 to 13 layers at high displacement fields where the conduction band minimum is nearly flat with near-ideal quantum geometry. In this regime, the authors observe the formation of a "quarter metal"—a spontaneously spin- and valley-polarized state in which the electron liquid occupies a single flavor—and find that the local orbital magnetization peaks at finite carrier density rather than at the charge-neutrality point. This density dependence is quantitatively consistent with Berry curvature concentrated on a finite-momentum "ring of fire" in the Brillouin zone, directly linking the measured magnetization to the topological quantum geometry of the band structure. The results provide real-space confirmation of theoretically predicted orbital ferromagnetism in moiré-free 2D graphene and offer a new experimental probe of the quantum geometric tensor in flat-band 2D materials. Importantly, these measurements shed light on the relationship between the quarter-metal orbital order and the chiral superconducting state observed at nearby densities, advancing the understanding of competing phases in rhombohedral multilayer graphene.

### Absolute measurement of penetration depth of superconducting thin films using microwave stripline resonators ([2605.28759](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.28759))

Extracting the absolute London penetration depth λ from microwave resonator measurements has remained a longstanding challenge because standard single-resonator approaches yield only relative changes in λ, not its absolute value. This paper by Dutta et al. resolves this problem through a hybrid method that combines temperature-dependent microwave stripline resonator measurements with full finite-element electromagnetic simulations of the resonator geometry, allowing the absolute λ to be determined self-consistently across a wide temperature range. The method is demonstrated on NbN superconducting thin films, yielding penetration depth values consistent with independent bulk estimates and providing a complete λ(T) curve from near Tc down to low temperatures. Because microwave stripline resonators are widely used as building blocks for superconducting qubits and kinetic inductance detectors, the ability to extract absolute λ directly from resonator characterization data—without separate calibration samples or DC measurements—is highly significant for quantum circuit engineering and materials optimization.
