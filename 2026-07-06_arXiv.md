## Extended Summaries: 2D Materials & Microwave Engineering

### Transition-Metal Tailored Ga₂O₂ Monolayer: From Room-Temperature Gas Sensing to Chemical Scavenging (2607.02012)

Pristine Ga₂O₂ monolayers exhibit poor sensitivity and weak molecular capture, limiting their use in gas detection and environmental remediation. Haque and Singha address this by performing comprehensive first-principles DFT calculations on Ga₂O₂ monolayers substituted with seven transition metals (Pd, Zn, Zr, Mo, Ag, Ti, and Pt) and evaluating their performance against eight toxic industrial chemicals and three environmental gases. All TM-substituted monolayers display negative formation and binding energies, negligible lattice distortion, and confirmed structural stability in molecular dynamics simulations, ruling out degradation issues. Performance evaluation reveals a rich functional diversity: certain dopants yield selective, reusable, room-temperature gas sensors while others produce permanent molecular scavengers, enabling material-by-design approaches for toxic gas monitoring and air purification. This systematic screening of a 2D oxide monolayer platform offers a roadmap for engineering gas-sensing 2D materials across a broad range of target analytes and functional requirements.

### Disorder-induced superconductivity in graphene (2607.02267)

This work challenges the conventional understanding that disorder suppresses superconductivity, demonstrating instead that disorder can be the primary driver of a superconducting state in a 2D material. Van Poppelen, Löthman, and Black-Schaffer show that low concentrations of vacancies or hydrogenated sites in monolayer graphene generate a high density of localized low-energy states near the Dirac point, which effectively mediates Cooper pairing for any finite attractive electron-electron interaction. For conventional s-wave symmetry, the induced order parameter is finite even in the limit of arbitrarily weak interactions—behavior qualitatively distinct from BCS theory for pristine systems. The finding is significant for the 2D materials community because it implies that nominally defective graphene samples could naturally host superconducting correlations, potentially explaining transport anomalies in certain substrate-supported or irradiated graphene devices. More broadly, it establishes a disorder-driven superconducting mechanism that may apply to other Dirac semimetal monolayers.
