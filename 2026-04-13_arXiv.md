## Extended Summaries: 2D Materials & Microwave Engineering

### Physics-Informed 3D Atomic Reconstruction and Dynamics of Free-Standing Graphene from Single Low-Dose TEM Images — [2604.07271](https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.07271)
Resolving the three-dimensional atomic geometry of free-standing graphene in real time is essential for understanding how intrinsic rippling governs its electronic properties, yet the low electron doses required to avoid radiation damage impose severe signal-to-noise constraints on conventional reconstruction methods. Zhang et al. present a physics-informed computational framework that reconstructs 3D atomic coordinates of single-layer graphene from individual low-dose TEM frames (8×10³ e⁻/Å², 1 ms temporal resolution), an unprecedented combination of dose efficiency and temporal resolution. The method integrates physical constraints from graphene's bonding geometry with machine-learning-aided image processing to extract reliable 3D positions despite the low signal. This approach enables direct, real-time observation of out-of-plane rippling dynamics and their coupling to electronic structure, opening a path toward understanding how thermally driven atomic fluctuations modulate the electronic, mechanical, and thermal properties of 2D materials.

### Microscopic evidence of spin-driven multiferroicity and topological spin textures in monolayer NiI₂ — [2604.06959](https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.06959)
Monolayer NiI₂ is a van der Waals material hosting a rare combination of magnetic order and electric polarization (type-II multiferroicity), but direct atomic-scale evidence for its spin-driven mechanism has been lacking. Using vectorial spin-polarized scanning tunneling microscopy, Wang et al. identify a canted spin-spiral state in monolayer NiI₂ with a fully determined spin rotation plane, accompanied by a 2Q charge modulation consistent with spin-driven ferroelectric polarization. At spin-spiral domain walls, the authors discover topological spin textures composed of meron/antimeron pairs that are associated with distinct charge patterns and measurable band shifts, providing direct signatures of local bound charges induced by variations in ferroelectric polarization at the domain boundaries. These findings establish monolayer NiI₂ as a platform for studying the interplay between topological spin textures and ferroelectricity at the atomic scale in a truly 2D van der Waals system.

### Light-controlled van der Waals tunnel junctions: mechanisms, architectures, functionalities, and opportunities — [2604.08399](https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.08399)
Van der Waals heterostructures with atomically thin barriers enable a new class of tunnel junctions where optical excitation drives nonequilibrium carrier populations and opens transport pathways inaccessible in equilibrium, making them powerful platforms for studying ultrafast and collective quantum phenomena. Shehabeldin et al. provide a comprehensive review of the fundamental mechanisms of photo-assisted tunneling in vdW systems, covering how engineered band alignment, clean interfaces, and highly tunable 2D barriers uniquely combine with optical control. The review describes emerging device functionalities — including broadband photodetection, tunneling-driven electroluminescence, optical sensing, and nonvolatile optical memory — that exploit the interplay between tunneling and light–matter interaction in 2D materials. A forward-looking perspective identifies quantum-geometric probes, twist-resolved spectroscopy, and moiré ferroelectricity as particularly promising directions, with scalable architectures for computing and sensing as longer-term goals.

### Valley-controlled many-body exciton interactions in monolayer WSe₂ phototransistors — [2604.08382](https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.08382)
Many-body interactions among excitons are central to the nonlinear optical response of transition metal dichalcogenides (TMDs), yet controlling these interactions optically has remained largely unexplored. Vaquero et al. demonstrate all-optical control of many-body exciton interactions in monolayer WSe₂ using valley-selective excitation: by choosing the circular polarization of a pulsed laser, they selectively populate one valley and use polarization-resolved photocurrent spectroscopy in a phototransistor geometry to read out the resulting changes in exciton–exciton interactions. The technique distinguishes between intravalley and intervalley scattering contributions and reveals how the valley degree of freedom provides an additional handle on the many-body exciton landscape. This valley-selective optical control of many-body interactions has direct implications for valleytronics and for the design of nonlinear optical components based on 2D TMD heterostructures.

### Mode-Resolved Multiband Ballistic Transport and Conductance Thresholds in Bilayer Graphene Junctions — [2604.07504](https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.07504)
Bilayer graphene's multiband electronic structure and tunable interlayer coupling make it an attractive platform for gate-controlled ballistic devices, but the interplay of symmetry constraints, interlayer bias, and mechanical strain on transmission has not been fully characterized. Liu, Zheng, and Pantaleon carry out a full four-band analysis of ballistic bilayer graphene junctions, showing that symmetry rules suppress transmission at specific incidence angles even when electronic states are available, and that an interlayer bias lifts this suppression through mode mixing while opening a tunable transport gap. Their key finding is a distinct conductance threshold marking the onset of propagation in the upper Bernal-stacked band inside the gated barrier, producing a characteristic change in the slope of the conductance that serves as an experimentally accessible fingerprint of interlayer coupling strength. The complementary roles of gating, interlayer bias, and homogeneous strain identified in this work provide a design framework for engineering high-performance ballistic transistors in bilayer graphene.
