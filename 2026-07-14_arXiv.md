## Extended Summaries: 2D Materials & Microwave Engineering

### [2607.09421] Short-range magnetic order and multi-stage phase transitions in the easy-plane van der Waals magnet CrCl₃
T. B. Mazitov, M. I. Panin, A. S. Pakhomov, M. V. Bakhmetiev, E. O. Chiglintsev, A. I. Chernov, A. A. Katanin

CrCl₃ is a prototypical 2D van der Waals magnet whose layered structure permits mechanical exfoliation to the monolayer limit, making it a key platform for studying magnetism in reduced dimensions. This work employs broadband ferromagnetic resonance (FMR) spectroscopy and DC SQUID magnetometry on mechanically exfoliated CrCl₃ microflakes in tandem with non-local DFT+DMFT calculations. The measurements confirm a two-step magnetic transition on cooling: a crossover from paramagnet to a spin-polarised phase, followed by the onset of long-range antiferromagnetic order, with robust short-range spin correlations persisting well above both critical temperatures. The DFT+DMFT calculations elucidate the role of quantum fluctuations and interlayer coupling in stabilising the multi-stage behaviour. These results establish CrCl₃ microflakes as a sensitive platform for probing critical spin fluctuations in 2D magnets and provide a comprehensive picture of the magnetic phase diagram relevant for spintronic device design.

### [2607.09256] Terahertz field-driven nonlinear Hall effect and other second-order transport phenomena in two-dimensional tellurene
M. D. Moldavskaya, L. E. Golub et al.

Two-dimensional tellurene is a low-symmetry semiconductor with strongly broken inversion and mirror symmetries, making it an ideal host for large second-order nonlinear transport effects. This study irradiates tellurene Hall-bar devices with linearly polarised terahertz pulses and measures the resulting dc photocurrent simultaneously along and perpendicular to the crystallographic c-axis. The nonlinear Hall effect signal scales quadratically with the in-plane THz electric field and shows the expected dependence on the polarisation direction, consistent with the symmetry-allowed second-order response tensors of 2D tellurene. By resolving both longitudinal and transverse photocurrent components, the authors disentangle contributions from Berry-curvature-dipole-driven nonlinear Hall effects and nonlinear Drude transport, providing direct experimental access to the quantum geometry of the material. The work demonstrates THz spectroscopy as a powerful tool for probing nonlinear quantum transport in 2D semiconductors with low crystal symmetry.

### [2607.06711] Moiré Phonon Condensation in Magic-Angle Twisted Bilayer Graphene
Zhanghao Zhouyin, Jyun-Jie Jiang, Xianghua Kong, Hong Guo (McGill University / Shenzhen University)

Twisted bilayer graphene at the magic angle (~1.08°) spontaneously reconstructs from a weakly corrugated breathing morphology into a strongly bending-dominated pattern, but the microscopic origin of this collective crossover had remained unexplained. This work proposes and validates a phonon soft-mode mechanism — Moiré Phonon Condensation (MPC) — whereby layer-symmetric A₁ moiré flexural phonons soften as the twist angle approaches the magic angle, eventually freezing into the bending configuration. Mode-resolved tight-binding calculations across 11164 atoms in the full moiré supercell show that the reconstructed geometry is captured by just two phonon eigenmodes at >99.5% spectral weight, confirming the single-mode-condensation picture. A first-harmonic continuum theory identifies a dimensionless control parameter (the ratio of moiré length scale to bending-to-breathing stiffness) that changes sign at the magic angle. The condensed phonon coordinates are found to be electronically active, linking the structural reconstruction directly to the formation of flat bands, offering a unified picture of why magic-angle physics is so sensitive to the precise twist angle.

### [2607.08585] Vortex Dynamics in Magic-Angle Twisted Graphene
Marta Perego, Peter Koopmann, Clara Galante Agero, Alexandra Mestre Torà et al. (ETH Zürich / NIMS Japan)

Twisted bilayer graphene at the magic angle is a rare example of a 2D superconductor in which vortex dynamics can be studied at extraordinarily low carrier densities and near the BKT transition. This work fabricates a gate-defined Josephson junction within a twisted-layer graphene device and uses the junction as a high-sensitivity single-vortex detector: each vortex entering or leaving the superconducting leads produces a discrete shift in the Fraunhofer critical-current pattern. Time traces of the junction voltage exhibit telegraph-type noise with two distinct timescales, attributed to rapid fluctuations of vortex-antivortex pairs in the leads modulated by slowly diffusing trapped vortices. At higher temperatures, vortex penetration rates follow an Arrhenius law consistent with thermal activation over energy barriers set by the Pearl length; at low temperatures the rate saturates in a manner quantitatively consistent with macroscopic quantum tunneling. The ability to detect and distinguish individual vortex events — including multi-vortex processes — makes this platform a versatile tool for probing 2D superconducting fluctuations in van der Waals heterostructures.

### [2607.09178] Tunable responsivity and bandwidth in microwave kinetic inductance detectors via readout current nonlinearity
M. Rouble (t0.technology, Montréal), P. Day (JPL), M. Dobbs (McGill University) et al.

Microwave kinetic inductance detectors (MKIDs) are superconducting resonators widely used in millimetre/sub-millimetre astronomy and quantum sensing, and their sensitivity is ultimately limited by the noise contribution of the first readout amplifier. This work exploits the bifurcation nonlinearity of superconducting resonators: when a readout tone is placed slightly below the resonant frequency at high power, the kinetic inductance nonlinearity creates a positive feedback that dramatically amplifies the detector response to both optical photons and microwave power. By tuning the readout power and frequency to just below the bifurcation point, the authors achieve a 10× enhancement in optical responsivity relative to the linear operating point, with a corresponding reduction in resonator bandwidth. A comprehensive map of tunable operational states — spanning responsivity-bandwidth trade-offs — is presented, allowing detector systems to be matched to specific observational or sensing requirements. This technique requires no changes to detector fabrication and can be applied to any MKID array, making it immediately relevant for next-generation millimetre-wave surveys and quantum microwave sensing applications.

### [2607.06520] Multi-Knob Switchable Chiral Superconductivity Quartet in Rhombohedral Graphene
Zhenqi Hua, Shenyong Ye, Phatthanon Pattanakanvijit et al.

Rhombohedral graphene multilayers host a flat band near charge neutrality that is uniquely susceptible to interaction-driven instabilities, including chiral (time-reversal-symmetry-breaking) superconductivity. This work identifies a quartet of four distinct chiral superconducting states in rhombohedral graphene that can be selected and switched by combinations of gate voltage, applied magnetic field, and electric displacement field — hence "multi-knob switchable." The switching between chiral states is controlled by the relative occupation of valley-polarised flat bands, and each state carries a distinct edge-current pattern and spontaneous orbital magnetisation that could be detected optically or magnetically. Theoretical modelling within a self-consistent mean-field approach captures the observed phase boundaries and identifies the interplay of Coulomb interaction, displacement field screening, and spin-valley locking as the key determinants of which chiral phase is stabilised. The results position rhombohedral graphene as a designable platform for chiral superconducting devices with electrically programmable topology.
