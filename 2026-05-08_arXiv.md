## Extended Summaries: 2D Materials & Microwave Engineering

### [2605.05199] Field-Induced Asymmetric Band Flattening and Ideal Quantum Geometry in Rhombohedral Graphene
*2D Materials | cond-mat.str-el, cond-mat.mes-hall, cond-mat.supr-con | H. Zhang, J. Lu, S. Wu, …, G. Chen, S. Zhou*

Rhombohedral multilayer graphene hosts a rich menagerie of correlated phases whose occurrence depends sensitively on the perpendicular displacement field, yet the origin of the pronounced electron-hole asymmetry across the phase diagram has lacked direct spectroscopic evidence. Zhang et al. perform angle-resolved photoemission spectroscopy (ARPES) on rhombohedral graphene devices equipped with electrostatic gates, systematically tracking the flat conduction band (FCB) and flat valence band (FVB) as a function of applied field. Under large displacement fields the FVB develops a characteristic M-shaped dispersion indicative of a competing exchange interaction, while the FCB progressively flattens toward a dispersionless limit and simultaneously develops finite Berry curvature and a Fubini-Study metric close to the saturation (ideal) value. Combining the ARPES data with first-principles calculations, the authors show that both the band flatness and the quantum-geometric ideal condition are optimized within the same displacement-field window where experiments have observed the fractional quantum anomalous Hall effect exclusively on the electron-doped side. This work provides direct spectroscopic evidence linking the electronic structure of a 2D graphene multilayer to the conditions that favor strongly interacting topological phases, firmly establishing rhombohedral graphene as a platform where band geometry and correlations can be tuned in tandem.

### [2605.04871] A Two-Step CVD Process for the Synthesis of an Ir(111)/Borophene/2D-hBN Heterostructure by Intrinsic Segregation
*2D Materials | cond-mat.mtrl-sci | M. A. Kriegel et al.*

Borophene—the atomically thin allotrope of boron—has attracted interest for its predicted anisotropic Dirac-like dispersion and metallic character, but integrating borophene into technologically relevant heterostructures with encapsulating 2D layers has required complex sequential growth steps. Kriegel and collaborators report a streamlined two-step UHV-CVD protocol using borazine (B₃N₃H₆) as a single molecular precursor on an Ir(111) substrate that exploits the finite but temperature-dependent solubility of boron in iridium. In step one, low borazine pressure at high temperature deposits nitrogen-free boron atoms that dissolve into the near-surface Ir lattice, building a boron reservoir; in step two, higher borazine pressure at the same temperature drives hBN monolayer formation on the Ir surface. During controlled cooldown, the reduced boron solubility of Ir spontaneously drives dissolved boron to segregate to the surface, intercalating beneath the hBN overlayer and forming a borophene monolayer. Spot-profile analysis LEED, LEEM, and STM measurements confirm that the resulting Ir(111)/borophene/hBN heterostructure achieves micron-scale grain uniformity over the entire substrate. This intrinsic segregation-driven route avoids the need for separate borophene growth and transfer steps, offering a scalable strategy for incorporating borophene into 2D heterostructure stacks where hBN serves as a protective encapsulant or tunnel barrier.

### [2605.03766] Gossamer Superconductivity in Moiré WSe₂ Bilayer
*2D Materials | cond-mat.supr-con | H.-K. Jin, G. Ji, Z. Wang, J. Wang, F.-C. Zhang*

The recent observation of robust superconductivity at half-filling in twisted bilayer WSe₂ has reinvigorated debate about whether moiré TMD systems are closer to BCS metals or to doped Mott insulators in their pairing mechanism. Jin and coworkers propose that the twisted WSe₂ system occupies an intermediate regime they term "gossamer superconductivity": the Coulomb repulsion is strong enough to partially suppress double occupancy (reducing the doublon density to a small but finite value) but not strong enough to drive full Mott localization, so the remaining mobile doublons and holes bind into Cooper pairs via an effective attractive interaction mediated by virtual doublon-hole fluctuations. They map the moiré continuum Hamiltonian of twisted WSe₂ onto a triangular-lattice extended Hubbard model and solve the strong-coupling phase diagram using renormalized mean-field theory, finding a gossamer superconducting dome at half-filling flanked by correlated metallic and insulating regions. The resulting s±-wave order parameter, small superfluid stiffness, and dome-shaped Tc vs. doping curve quantitatively match experimental observations—including the sensitivity of Tc to displacement field—while providing a physical mechanism distinct from both pure BCS pairing and cuprate-like spin-fluctuation mediation. This gossamer picture positions twisted WSe₂ as a model system for studying the crossover between Fermi-liquid superconductivity and Mott-driven pairing in an electrostatically tunable 2D material.

### [2605.06146] Sub-Kelvin Thermal Conductivity of Substrates and On-Chip Routing in Quantum Integrated Systems
*Microwave Engineering (cryogenic quantum systems) | cond-mat.mes-hall, cond-mat.mtrl-sci | C. Bon-Mardion et al.*

Superconducting quantum processors operate at millikelvin temperatures where microwave signal routing and thermalization of on-chip components must satisfy stringent thermal budgets that are difficult to predict from room-temperature material data. Bon-Mardion and colleagues systematically measure the thermal conductivity of several candidate substrate materials and on-chip microwave routing conductors at temperatures from 10 mK to 1 K, covering the operating range of dilution-refrigerator-based quantum systems. They find that thermal conductivity varies significantly between substrate materials (silicon, sapphire, and alternative insulators) in this regime, with phonon mean-free-path effects producing strong deviations from bulk predictions and substrate-to-substrate differences exceeding an order of magnitude below 100 mK. The on-chip routing wiring (normal-metal and superconducting traces) shows additional anomalies linked to two-level-system (TLS) coupling and electron-phonon decoupling. The measured values are directly applicable to the design of multi-qubit processor tiles where localized heating from readout pulses and parasitic dissipation in microwave feedlines must be managed to maintain qubit coherence, making this work an important materials reference for the quantum computing engineering community.
