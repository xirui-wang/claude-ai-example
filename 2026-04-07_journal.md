## Extended Summaries: 2D Materials & Microwave Engineering

### [Angle Evolution of the Superconducting Phase Diagram in Twisted Bilayer WSe₂](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-026-10357-2)
This study systematically fabricated and measured twisted bilayer WSe₂ devices spanning a range of twist angles (3.65°–5.0°) to resolve a controversy between two earlier experiments that reported seemingly inconsistent phase diagrams. Using transport measurements compared against twist-angle-dependent theories, the authors find superconductivity persists across the entire angle range and in all cases is proximal to a Fermi surface reconstruction, presumably antiferromagnetic in origin. The result neither pins superconductivity to the Van Hove singularity nor to a half-band Mott insulator state, but instead connects the two previously distinct phase diagrams and points to strong-coupling physics as the key driver. This establishes twisted TMD bilayers as a unified platform for studying correlation-driven superconductivity, with relevance to the broader class of moiré quantum materials.

### [Super-Moiré Spin Textures in Twisted Two-Dimensional Antiferromagnets](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41565-025-02103-y)
Using scanning nitrogen-vacancy (NV) magnetometry with sub-100 nm resolution, the authors image the magnetic texture in twisted double-bilayer chromium triiodide (CrI₃) at small twist angles. They find long-range magnetic order extending beyond a single moiré unit cell — a "super-moiré" magnetic state — in which the spontaneous magnetic texture size increases with twist angle, opposite to the behavior of the underlying moiré wavelength. At the optimal twist angle of ~1.1°, the magnetic texture reaches ~300 nm in extent, nearly an order of magnitude larger than the moiré wavelength, and vanishes for angles above 2°. The field maps are consistent with the formation of antiferromagnetic Néel-type skyrmions spanning multiple moiré cells, opening new pathways for engineering topological spin textures in 2D magnets.

### [Twelve-Inch Electrically Anisotropic Boridene for Optoelectronic Computing](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41565-026-02122-3)
Mo₄/₃B₂Tz (boridene), a 2D material with ordered metal vacancies, is grown by a low-temperature (< 150 °C) deposition process compatible with back-end-of-line semiconductor integration and demonstrated at the twelve-inch wafer scale with excellent uniformity. Unlike conventional 2D materials where in-plane conduction dominates, boridene exhibits an unusual anisotropy in which through-plane resistance is lower than in-plane, arising from the ordered vacancy channels. This anisotropy enables a simplified three-terminal device in which light and voltage jointly control a bipolar, highly linear memristive photoresponse — ideal for neuromorphic hardware. The work connects wafer-scale manufacturability with a fundamentally new electronic anisotropy mechanism in 2D materials, positioning boridene as a candidate for next-generation optoelectronic computing platforms.

### [Room-Temperature Two-Dimensional Multiferroic Metal with Voltage-Controllable Magnetic Order](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41563-026-02537-2)
Bilayer CrTe₂ is a van der Waals ferromagnetic metal, and this work reveals that in air-stable bilayer form it simultaneously carries both magnetic and ferroelectric order — a true 2D multiferroic metal. Structural and magnetic characterization demonstrates an alternating ferromagnetic/antiferromagnetic interlayer architecture that spontaneously breaks inversion symmetry and generates a switchable out-of-plane ferroelectric polarization via an interlayer charge-transfer mechanism distinct from conventional spin-orbit-coupling routes. Scanning probe microscopy confirms that an applied electric field non-volatilely switches between distinct magnetization states, enabling combined electrical-write/magnetic-read functionality. The discovery of multiferroicity rooted in charge transfer provides a new design principle for engineering multiferroic behavior in layered 2D van der Waals systems at ambient temperature, with direct relevance to spintronic memory and logic devices.

### [Femtosecond Concerted Rotation of Molecules on a 2D Material Interface](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-69801-6)
The study employs a multimodal time-resolved photoemission approach that simultaneously tracks valence electronic states, core-level binding energies, and orbital wavefunctions at femtosecond time resolution and sub-Ångström spatial precision. Upon photoexcitation, rapid charge transfer from the 2D material substrate to an adsorbed molecular overlayer is detected; this modifies the intermolecular forces and gives rise to a macroscopic, unidirectional concerted rotation of the entire molecular adlayer within hundreds of femtoseconds. The analysis disentangles intertwined electronic-structural dynamics of both the molecule and the 2D substrate, demonstrating that photoinduced charge redistribution reshapes the interfacial energy potential to create a transient homochiral state. This result reveals a previously unknown ultrafast light-driven collective chirality induction mechanism at hybrid organic/2D material interfaces, with implications for ultrafast optoelectronics and photochemical switching devices based on 2D platforms.

### [Extreme Longitudinal Thermal Conductivity and Non-Diffusive Heat Transport in Isotopic hBN](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-69907-x)
Monoisotopically purified ¹⁰B hexagonal boron nitride (h¹⁰BN) is suspended in a microbridge geometry allowing simultaneous resistance-thermometry and Raman thermometry to probe in-plane heat transport. At room temperature, the measured thermal conductivity exceeds 1,650 W m⁻¹ K⁻¹, dramatically higher than previously reported values for hBN and placing h¹⁰BN among the highest thermally conducting materials known. Below ~300 K, the temperature profile deviates sharply from linearity, indicating that phonon transport transitions from the diffusive (Fourier) regime into a non-diffusive regime in which phonons travel ballistically or hydrodynamically over length scales comparable to the sample size. These findings call for a new theoretical framework describing heat transport in ultrapure 2D materials and have practical significance for thermal management using hBN in 2D electronic devices.

### [Hyperbolic Localized Plasmons and Twist-Induced Chirality in MoOCl₂](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-69435-8)
MoOCl₂ is a strongly in-plane anisotropic van der Waals crystal that supports hyperbolic phonon polaritons; here, disk-shaped nanostructures are fabricated by nanopatterning to create localized plasmonic resonances that inherit the hyperbolic character of the bulk material. These hyperbolic localized plasmon resonances (H-LPRs) display three hallmark properties absent in conventional plasmons: resonance selectivity along only one in-plane axis in symmetric disk arrays, independence of the resonance frequency from gap in MoOCl₂–insulator–metal stacks, and emergence of optical chirality upon twisting of stacked MoOCl₂ layers without breaking geometric symmetry. A companion paper reports spatiotemporal imaging of long-range anisotropic plasmon polaritons in hyperbolic MoOCl₂, further establishing it as a rich platform for chiral nanophotonics and twist-tunable plasmonic devices. Collectively, these results have implications for polarization engineering and light-field control at the nanoscale.

### [Nanoscale Imaging of Magnetotransport Around a Circular p-n Junction in Graphene](https://journals.aps.org/prl/abstract/10.1103/3ml4-gr6h)
The experiment employs scanning tunneling potentiometry (STP) to image carrier flow in high-quality hBN-encapsulated graphene around electrostatic circular p-n junctions at magnetic fields from 0 to 1.4 T, mapping the electrochemical potential with ~1 nm resolution. At low fields, carriers diffuse around the barrier, producing a dipole-like potential pattern; at moderate fields, ohmic spiraling is observed; and at high fields, carriers flow in smooth cycloid-like orbits without backscattering despite expectations of ballistic bounce trajectories. Detailed modeling shows that the formation of quantized Landau levels suppresses the orbital degrees of freedom responsible for bouncing, forcing carriers into these coherent cyclotron trajectories. The results provide direct nanoscale visualization of quantum Hall edge-state physics around a circular geometry, connecting mesoscopic transport theory with real-space imaging in graphene.

### [Reentrant Superconductivity in a Josephson Junction Array Tuned by Radio-Frequency Power](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-71256-8)
Granular aluminum consists of superconducting aluminum grains separated by thin oxide barriers, forming a self-assembled Josephson junction array; in this study, the application of radiofrequency (RF) power in the microwave range drives the system through reentrant superconductivity — an unusual state in which superconductivity re-emerges after being suppressed by temperature or magnetic field. At low temperatures, large Shapiro steps appear with characteristics of a single coherent junction despite the array's many junctions, indicating that coherent phase locking across thousands of junctions amplifies the quantized voltage response. The RF-induced re-entrance is attributed to charge screening — a many-body correlation effect where the RF field effectively reduces Coulomb repulsion between Cooper pairs, restoring phase coherence. The quantized voltage amplification and tunability by RF frequency and power suggest this natural Josephson array as a compact platform for microwave-referenced voltage standards and cryogenic microwave detectors.
