## Extended Summaries: 2D Materials & Microwave Engineering

### Super-moiré spin textures in twisted two-dimensional antiferromagnets
**Nature Nanotechnology (2026)** — King Cho Wong et al. (Univ. of Stuttgart)

Twisting two atomically thin antiferromagnetic layers out of registry creates a moiré pattern that modulates both the exchange coupling and magnetic anisotropy on the nanoscale; stacking a second moiré period (super-moiré) generates an even richer magnetic landscape that has remained experimentally unexplored. The Stuttgart team employed a scanning NV-center magnetometer, which senses stray fields with nanoscale spatial resolution and single-spin sensitivity, to image the spin texture of twisted 2D antiferromagnetic bilayers across the super-moiré unit cell. They discovered a hierarchical magnetic structure—spin textures that vary on both the primary moiré and the super-moiré length scales—constituting a previously unknown form of 2D magnetism. The magnetic signals are extremely weak (sub-μT stray fields), making NV-center quantum sensing the enabling technology: conventional magnetometry would be blind to this phase. Beyond fundamental interest, these super-moiré spin textures could encode topologically protected magnetic bits accessible via purely electrical control through moiré-tuned exchange coupling.

### Orbital-dependent Coulomb drag in electron-hole bilayer graphene heterostructures
**Phys. Rev. Lett. 136, 136701 (2026)** — Zuocheng Zhang et al.

Coulomb drag—the frictional momentum transfer between spatially separated charge layers through long-range Coulomb interactions—has long been used to probe interlayer correlations, but its orbital degree of freedom in graphene multilayers has not been systematically studied. Zhang et al. fabricated high-quality electron-hole bilayer graphene heterostructures separated by a thin hBN spacer and performed drag measurements as a function of carrier density, temperature, and layer displacement field. They find that the drag resistivity is strongly orbital-character dependent: it is dramatically enhanced when one or both layers are tuned to flat-band moiré states with enhanced orbital degeneracy, a signature that cannot be explained by conventional dielectric screening. The results identify Coulomb drag as an orbital-resolved probe of layer-locked electronic correlations and interlayer exciton formation in 2D heterostructures. These findings are directly relevant to the design of interlayer-correlated devices where orbital flavor adds an additional tuning handle beyond spin and valley.

### Reentrant superconductivity in a naturally occurring Josephson junction array tuned by radio-frequency power
**Nature Communications (2026)** — [s41467-026-71256-8](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-71256-8)

Granular aluminum thin films, in which nanoscale superconducting grains are weakly coupled through an amorphous oxide matrix, naturally realize a disordered Josephson junction array—a canonical model for studying quantum phase transitions between superconducting and insulating states. This work demonstrates that applying radio-frequency (microwave-frequency) power to a granular aluminum nanobridge drives a reentrant superconductor-to-insulator crossover: a sample that has been driven into the insulating state by gate voltage recovers superconductivity under sufficient RF drive, and the phenomenon is reversible. The reentrance is attributed to RF-driven suppression of quantum phase fluctuations that are responsible for destroying phase coherence in the insulating regime, providing direct evidence that microwave photons can act as an effective thermal bath that restores global superconducting order in a disordered array. These results establish radio-frequency power as a handle for controlling quantum phase transitions in superconducting nanostructures, with immediate relevance to microwave engineering of superconducting circuits and quantum computing elements.
