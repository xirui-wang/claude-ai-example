## Extended Summaries: 2D Materials & Microwave Engineering

### Atomic scale demonstration of ferromagnetism in a single layer FeCl₂ on Au(111) — [2605.22783](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.22783)

Candia et al. report the first atomic-scale confirmation of ferromagnetic order in a monolayer of FeCl₂ grown by molecular beam epitaxy on an Au(111) substrate, using spin-polarized scanning tunnelling microscopy (SP-STM) as the primary probe. The monolayer exhibits a wide insulating gap of 3.3 eV and a strongly spin-polarized conduction band emerging 1.5 eV above the Fermi level, together with triangular atomic-scale defects that play a primary role in modulating the electronic gap and spin density distribution. This work represents a significant step for the field of 2D magnetic materials, as FeCl₂ monolayers combine insulating character, sizable magnetic susceptibility, and ease of MBE growth on multiple substrates — properties highly desirable for integration into van der Waals heterostructure devices. The atomic-resolution characterization establishes FeCl₂ as a robust single-layer ferromagnet, complementing the handful of confirmed 2D ferromagnets such as CrI₃ and Fe₃GeTe₂, and opens pathways for electric-field control of magnetism in all-2D device stacks.

### Transport Enhancement and In Situ Control of Electronic Correlation via Photoinduced Modulation Doping of van der Waals Heterostructures — [2605.22452](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.22452)

This paper demonstrates a straightforward, all-optical technique for modulation doping in hBN-encapsulated graphene heterostructures, exploiting standard white-light illumination — without any additional device fabrication — to populate trap states at the SiO₂ interface and thereby dope the graphene channel in a controlled, reversible manner. By systematically analyzing transport data with Drude and Landauer models, the authors quantitatively track how illumination duration tunes both carrier density and charge disorder, enabling continuous switching between diffusive and quasi-ballistic transport regimes in the same device. Crucially, the technique allowed the team to stabilize quantum Hall isospin ferromagnetic states that require very low disorder, underscoring the ability to reach high-quality electronic phases on demand without cool-down cycles. The significance for 2D materials research is broad: photoinduced modulation doping provides a clean, post-fabrication handle on electronic correlations in any van der Waals heterostructure that incorporates an oxide substrate, and its reversibility makes it an appealing tool for studying interaction-driven phases.

### Competing incommensurability, electronic correlations, and superconductivity in a hybrid transition metal dichalcogenide — [2605.22522](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.22522)

Souza et al. investigate the layered polytype 4Hb-TaS₂, which alternates metallic 1H-TaS₂ and correlated Mott-insulating 1T-TaS₂ layers and hosts bulk superconductivity, through a combined DFT and dynamical mean-field theory (DMFT) framework. The key discovery is that a previously overlooked incommensurate potential — arising from the natural lattice mismatch between the 1T and 1H layers — locally modulates the interlayer distance, thereby spatially varying both hybridization strength and the degree of charge transfer from the correlated 1T layer to the metallic 1H layer. This incommensurability competes with, and partially suppresses, the emergence of bulk superconductivity, explaining experimental observations of an anomalously low Tc relative to pure 1H-TaS₂. The results establish incommensurate inter-sublattice potentials as an important and largely unexplored ingredient in the physics of hybrid van der Waals polytypes, with implications for engineering correlated-electron and superconducting phases in designed stacking sequences of 2D materials.
