## Extended Summaries: 2D Materials & Microwave Engineering

### [2607.17129] STEP: Spin Tensor Equivariant Potential for Magnetic MLIPs — applied to monolayer CrI₃
Monolayer chromium triiodide (CrI₃) is a prototypical 2D ferromagnet whose rich magnetic phase diagram and magnon spectrum make it a prime benchmark for magnetic machine-learning interatomic potentials (MLIPs). STEP (Spin Tensor Equivariant Potential) represents the key advance of treating the magnetic moment **m** as a continuous geometric object by embedding it in an SO(3)-equivariant tensor framework, rather than discretizing spin states as in prior magnetic MLIPs. Higher-order tensor channels couple the center atom's spin to its chemical environment iteratively, enabling the model to learn orientation-dependent exchange interactions, single-ion anisotropy, and magnetoelastic coupling simultaneously. Benchmarks on monolayer CrI₃ demonstrate markedly improved data efficiency — reaching target accuracy with several-fold fewer training configurations than conventional approaches — making STEP a promising route toward spin-dynamics-coupled MD for complex 2D magnets and van der Waals heterostructures.

### [2607.18183] Excitonic effects in the photocarrier dynamics of two-dimensional materials
Two-dimensional transition-metal dichalcogenides (TMDs) like WSe₂ host strongly bound excitons that profoundly reshape ultrafast photocarrier dynamics, yet most carrier-dynamics formalisms treat photoexcited electrons and holes as independent quasiparticles. Betancur, Stefanucci, and Perfetto address this gap by deriving excitonic Bloch equations (XBE) that self-consistently incorporate carrier-phonon scattering and exciton formation within a nonequilibrium Green's function framework. Applied to non-resonantly pumped WSe₂ monolayers, XBE predict that thermalized carrier momentum distributions are sculpted by exciton wavefunctions rather than following Fermi-Dirac statistics — a distinction measurable by time- and angle-resolved photoemission spectroscopy (trARPES). The work, published in Nano Letters 2026, provides a computationally tractable first-principles description of exciton-mediated relaxation kinetics in 2D semiconductors, with direct implications for the design of ultrafast valleytronic and optoelectronic devices based on TMD monolayers.

### [2607.18214] Position-dependent tight-binding model for Li impurities in monolayer and bilayer graphene
Lithium functionalization of graphene is of central importance for Li-ion battery anodes and for inducing superconductivity via intercalation, yet the local electronic perturbation from adsorbed or intercalated Li atoms is poorly captured by uniform tight-binding models. This work develops a position-dependent tight-binding model in which hopping parameters and on-site energies vary continuously as a function of the Li adatom's in-plane position, parametrized by DFT calculations across a dense grid of adsorption sites. The model correctly reproduces the Li-induced local density of states redistribution in both monolayer and AB-stacked bilayer graphene and offers an efficient route to large-scale simulations of disordered or high-density Li coverage, relevant to charge-storage and intercalation physics in 2D carbon systems.

### [2607.17992] A Voltage-Controlled Josephson Frequency Comb
Josephson frequency combs operating in the microwave domain are of fundamental importance for quantum metrology, readout of superconducting qubits, and coherent multi-tone microwave sources, but existing comb architectures rely on passive circuit elements and lack direct tunability of individual comb modes. De Simoni and Giazotto propose a comb generator based on a Josephson field-effect transistor (JoFET) — a gate-tunable ballistic semiconductor-superconductor-semiconductor junction — embedded in a resistively-shunted circuit operating in the relaxation-oscillation (voltage-phase slipping) regime. The electrostatic gate provides continuous, in-situ control of the critical current Ic and hence the Josephson plasma frequency, directly setting the comb spacing and redistributing modal power across the spectrum without mechanical adjustments. The architecture is compatible with semiconductor-superconductor platforms (e.g., Al/InAs, Al/Ge) and could serve as a tunable microwave synthesizer integrated with transmon or spin qubit readout chains.

### [2607.18916] Remote Cooper Pairing via Multi-Hotspot Vacuum and Andreev Teleportation
Split-ring resonators (SRRs) are workhorse elements in microwave circuit quantum electrodynamics (cQED) whose field confinement is exploited here in an entirely new way: a multi-gap SRR geometry creates a single cavity photon with several spatially distinct deep-subwavelength hot spots separated by millimeter-scale distances, mediating effective many-body interactions between quantum materials placed at different gaps. Lin, Song, Xiao, and Yao demonstrate using DMRG and exact diagonalization that this remote vacuum-mediated coupling can produce Cooper pairing between electrons in superconductors that are macroscopically separated — a non-local form of proximity effect. Most remarkably, the geometry enables Andreev teleportation: injection of a hole into gap A coherently ejects an electron from gap B, analogous to quantum teleportation but realized via crossed Andreev reflection mediated by the common cavity mode. The proposal points toward a distributed microwave-QED architecture for entangling spatially separated superconducting or topological quantum systems.
