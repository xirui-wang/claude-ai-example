## Extended Summaries: 2D Materials & Microwave Engineering

### Phase-dependent electronic structure of two-dimensional Ag layers at the graphene/SiC interface — [2605.23855](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.23855)

Intercalation beneath epitaxial graphene on SiC offers a powerful route to stabilize atomically thin metal layers with properties inaccessible in bulk form. Datta, Zheng, Jain, Küster, Robinson, Crespi, and Starke characterize two distinct 2D silver phases—Ag(1), epitaxial to the SiC lattice and readily formed by conventional intercalation, and a denser Ag(2) phase previously only rarely observed—using ARPES, LEED, and in situ UHV preparation methods. The authors develop a controlled in situ procedure for selectively forming Ag(2), enabling systematic comparison of the two phases' electronic structures; they find markedly different band dispersions and Fermi surface topologies tied to the different packing densities and point-group symmetries. The sensitivity of the phase obtained to the starting substrate defect landscape underscores that engineering 2D metal intercalation requires precise control of the host surface chemistry. These results establish a framework for phase-selective preparation of 2D metal layers at graphene interfaces, opening new routes to tailor interfacial electronic properties in graphene heterostructures.

### Probing Dielectric Screening in van der Waals Heterostructures via Pressure-Tuned Exciton Rydberg Series — [2605.18506](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.18506)

The dielectric environment imposed by encapsulating layers profoundly governs exciton binding energies and optical selection rules in 2D transition-metal dichalcogenides. Badola, Smiri, Pelini, and co-workers employ low-temperature photoluminescence spectroscopy under hydrostatic pressure applied with a diamond anvil cell to track the excited-state Rydberg series of excitons in monolayer WSe₂ encapsulated in hexagonal boron nitride (hBN). As pressure increases, the Rydberg series is systematically compressed—a direct optical signature of enhanced Coulomb screening by the densifying hBN—allowing the authors to extract the dielectric constant of pressurized hBN as a function of applied load without any electrical contact. The methodology constitutes a new non-invasive all-optical technique for in situ dielectric characterization of few-layer van der Waals materials under extreme conditions, and provides quantitative benchmarks for theoretical descriptions of screening in vdW heterostructures. The approach is generalizable to other encapsulants and 2D semiconductors, and is relevant for designing pressure-tunable optoelectronic vdW devices.

### Optical Transmission of 2D Material with Quantum Anomalous Hall Effect — [2605.23211](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.23211)

The quantum anomalous Hall (QAH) effect in two-dimensional insulators—characterized by quantized Hall conductance and chiral edge states in zero external field—has attracted intense interest both for fundamental physics and for potential applications in topological photonics and dissipationless electronics. Pravda, Berman, and Ziegler develop an analytical theory of optical transmission through a gapped 2D material in the QAH phase, deriving closed-form expressions for the transmission, reflection, and absorption coefficients as universal functions of the ratio of incident photon energy to the topological gap energy. A key prediction is total reflection at the resonance condition (photon energy equal to gap energy), providing a distinctive optical fingerprint of the QAH gap. In the limit of vanishing gap, the theory correctly recovers the well-known universal optical conductance of graphene proportional to the fine-structure constant, validating the framework. These results offer quantitative predictions for optical spectroscopy of QAH insulators such as MnBi₂Te₄ and magnetically doped topological insulator thin films, and may guide the design of 2D-material-based topological photonic components.

### Spin-caloritronic signatures of soft magnons in bilayer CrSBr — [2605.18478](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.18478)

CrSBr is a layered van der Waals antiferromagnet with strong in-plane magnetic anisotropy and tightly bound excitons that has emerged as a versatile 2D platform for magnon-mediated spin transport. Den Teuling et al. calculate the magnon spin angular momentum in bilayer CrSBr as a function of applied magnetic field and wave vector, finding that the combination of triaxial magnetic anisotropy and intralayer dipolar interactions causes the magnon spin angular momentum to deviate substantially from the conventional ℏ and to diverge as the applied field drives the magnon dispersion toward a softening instability. This divergence generates a pronounced peak in the spin Seebeck coefficient—the caloritronic voltage produced by a thermal gradient across the magnet—providing a sharp, experimentally accessible spin-caloritronic signature of the proximity to the magnon-softening transition. The findings establish thermal spin transport as a sensitive probe of near-critical spin fluctuations in 2D magnets and suggest that the giant Seebeck anomaly could serve as a practical sensor of field-tunable magnetic phase boundaries in CrSBr-based heterostructures.
