## Extended Summaries: 2D Materials & Microwave Engineering

### On-chip superconducting GHz RF reflectometry of the capacitance response in bilayer graphene
**[2606.14431](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.14431)** | Sung Jin An et al.

This paper demonstrates a powerful integration of a 2D van der Waals heterostructure with superconducting microwave circuitry, directly addressing both 2D materials physics and microwave engineering. The authors encapsulate bilayer graphene (BLG) in hexagonal boron nitride and couple it to an on-chip niobium lumped-element LC resonator, performing RF reflectometry near 4.25 GHz to simultaneously resolve the resistive (real) and capacitive (imaginary) parts of the complex admittance. A perpendicular displacement field opens a tunable band gap in the BLG, and the resonance-frequency shift is converted to an effective capacitance using an equivalent-circuit model, yielding direct access to the electronic compressibility as a function of both gate voltages. Finite-bias transport maps reveal a region of suppressed conductance that grows with displacement field, consistent with the field-induced gap. This work establishes on-chip superconducting RF reflectometry as a versatile probe of gapped 2D electron systems and constitutes a key step toward coupling graphene-based quantum dots to superconducting microwave resonators for circuit-QED experiments.

### Multistate Manipulation of Charge-Spin Conversion in Two-Dimensional Ferroelectric Bilayers
**[2606.14378](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.14378)** | Weiyi Pan, Xinyuan Jiang, Jaroslav Fabian

This study proposes a mechanism for nonvolatile, multistate electrical control of spin-charge interconversion in 2D ferroelectric bilayer systems—a critical capability for next-generation spintronic memory. Symmetry analysis shows that when the two layers carry parallel interlayer polarization, reversing the net electric polarization switches the sign of the Edelstein effect (current-induced spin accumulation), while switching to an antiparallel configuration instead suppresses the Edelstein effect to zero while leaving the bulk spin Hall effect tunable. Three distinct states of spin accumulation thus become accessible through purely electrical polarization switching, without the need for magnetic fields or magnetic materials. The spin Hall effect channel is independently tunable across the parallel and antiparallel configurations, providing additional degrees of freedom for ternary logic operations. This framework offers a concrete materials design strategy for 2D-ferroelectric-based spintronic devices compatible with existing van der Waals heterostructure fabrication techniques.

### Enhanced superconductivity in atomically thin noble metals: From quantum confinement to interface-induced Lifshitz transition
**[2606.03663](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.03663)** | Chun-Jie Zhang et al.

This work addresses the challenge of inducing superconductivity in intrinsically non-superconducting noble metals by exploiting quantum confinement in the atomic-layer limit—a problem at the frontier of 2D materials and thin-film physics. The authors find that superconductivity is element-specific: Ag remains non-superconducting due to its stiff phonon spectrum, while Cu trilayers (Tc ≈ 0.78 K) and Au pentalayers (Tc ≈ 0.63 K) exhibit emergent superconductivity driven respectively by confinement-enhanced density of states and phonon softening. Crucially, the interfacial configuration in h-BN/Cu(111) heterostructures critically controls Tc, demonstrating the importance of substrate-engineering and Lifshitz transitions induced at the interface. These findings reveal that quantum confinement and interface-proximity effects together provide a knob for amplifying marginal superconducting tendencies in non-superconducting elemental metals, opening a design route for 2D superconducting devices using earth-abundant materials.
